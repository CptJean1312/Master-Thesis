ABSTRACT  
Floods in Germany have caused significant economic damage and are expected to become more frequent. The 
economic damage expected as a result of floods will affect households differently across income levels. A 
method, rooted in economic theory, is proposed to estimate changes in household welfare and income inequality 
resulting from expected flood damage. It is found that welfare losses after floods disproportionately harm low 
income households and increase inequality by 0.14%. Families with dependent children and householders of 
retirement age in the lowest income quartile are the most affected. In order to collect additional resources to 
cover the cost of the financial relief, the of additional income, real estate and energy taxes are investigated. 
These instruments yield a reduction in income inequality. It is also shown that rewarding investment in miti
-
gation and insurance, and supporting low income households can be jointly achieved when re-allocating ad-
ditional energy-tax revenues.   
1. Introduction 
In 2013, floods caused economic damage of approx. Eight billion 
euros in Germany (BMI 2013). The total compensation provided by the 
federal government accounted for 1.6% of its budget. While there are 
several studies on the distributional effects of natural disasters, there is 
little research on the effects of floods. Yamamura (2015) found that 
floods can increase inequality, but this effect vanishes after 10 years. 
Regarding existing literature on natural disasters and inequality, re
-
search shows that pre-existing inequality levels determine the ability of 
vulnerable households to respond immediately to the disaster and to 
recover from the event (see Masozera et al., 2007
). Miljkovic and 
Miljkovic (2014) found that hurricanes have heterogeneous impacts 
across different segments of the population. Similarly, Warr and Aung 
(2019) found that the cyclone Nargis, which affected Myanmar in 2008, 
decreased inequality across regions because the affected regions were 
on average better off than other regions. Keerthiratne and Tol (2018) 
also show that natural disasters in Sri Lanka affected poorer households 
working in the agricultural sector. Experiencing extreme events affects 
multiple dimensions of well-being. Yun and Waldorf (2016) show that 
hurricanes in the United States (U.S.) have increased inequality among 
the population in the coastal area and have forced households to mi
-
grate to other locations. Bui et al. (2014) show that natural disasters 
have a negative impact on income and expenditure levels, increasing 
income inequality. Sekulova and van den Bergh (2013, 2016) ; Hudson 
et al. (2019) and von Möllendorff and Hirschfeld (2016) have in-
vestigated the effects of extreme events on other dimensions outside of 
changes in expenditure and consumption, such as changes in life sa-
tisfaction and happiness. 
Floods will become more frequent (see Jongman et al., 2014
), in-
creasing the pressure on public finances. The Organization for Eco-
nomic Cooperation and Development (OECD) has suggested estab-
lishing a budget to cover the cost of future extreme events (see Phaup 
and Kirschner, 2010
). Cevik and Huang (2018) suggest that it is im-
portant to define how to build the fund and set the rules for its use. On 
the one hand, offering financial relief can create a charity hazard pro-
blem where households do not insure themselves, with the expectation 
that the government will provide financial assistance after the extreme 
event (see Raschky and Weck-Hannemann, 2007
). On the other hand, 
implementation of the wrong instruments to raise additional resources 
and their use can aggravate the distributional consequences of the ex
-
treme event. When extreme events become more frequent and in-
surance premiums reflect the risk level faced by households, some 
households will not be able to insure themselves, increasing the burden 
for low income households. To the best of my knowledge, this is the 
first study that uses an approach rooted in welfare economics to mea-
sure welfare changes at household and aggregated level.

household types and income levels is provided. Second, the distribu-

mechanisms is analyzed. Finally, the distributional effects of house

this scenario, revenues are generated by energy taxes and, the addi-

Countries such as Armenia, Indonesia, Japan, Philippines and South 
Africa have set public funds to face the cost of extreme events that 
range from 0.36% to 5% of the central government expenditure (see  
Cevik and Huang, 2018). Deviating public resources from education, 
health, etc. to create this fund can translate into reductions in non-cash 
income that can increase inequality (see Aaberge et al., 2010). Using 
taxes to finance funds against extreme events has already been im-
plemented in some countries. The Austrian disaster fund (Katas-
trophenfonds) is financed by 1.1% of the federal share on income taxes, 
taxes on capital yield, and corporate taxes (see Unterberger et al., 
2019
). There are also proposals to link sustainable consumption and 
adaption policies. For instance, using revenues from carbon taxes to 
finance an international-adaptation fund has been proposed by Chancel 
and Piketty (2015). de Villemeur and Leroux (2019) have suggested 
creating a climate liability market where liable polluters pay, as climate 
harm materializes. Members of this international market that face 
losses are compensated with these funds. However, energy taxes will 
also create a larger burden to low income households (see Poterba, 
1991; West, 2004, 2005; Fullerton and West, 2000). In order to increase 
the social acceptability of this policy instrument, re-allocation of the 
revenue has been suggested (see Klenert et al., 2018). In addition,  
Kousky and Kunreuther (2014) have analyzed the trade-off between 
setting insurance premiums that reflect the risk and equity and af-
fordability issues. Setting a premium that reflects risks could create a 
high burden for low income households. They proposed to combine a 
means-tested voucher to cover the cost of the premium, and investment 
in mitigation measures. In the same line, in order to make insurance 
more affordable Gruber (2008) suggests a financial framework where 
individuals or institutions acting as insurers can be sponsored by other 
entities. In this framework private and public companies work together 
to reduce the financial burden of compensation after an extreme event. 
In order to reduce the pressure on the public budget caused by damages 
to public buildings in Austria, Unterberger et al. (2019) suggest a switch 
from the current system of governmental relief towards a system where 
the federal government acts as insurer for the state members and im
-
plementation of insurance compensation arrangements offer incentives 
for risk reduction. Holub and Fuchs (2008) suggest that local invest-
ment in structural protection, building codes, and information cam-
paigns can also reduce the pressure on public budgets. 
Regarding the methodologies used to measure the incidence of flood 
damage, existing research takes expected changes of income after ex-
periencing natural events as a proxy to measure welfare. Kind et al. 
(2017) argue that the concept of utility used in economics to oper-
ationalise well-being cannot be approximated by income because well- 
being is non-linearly related to income or consumption. Demand sys-
tems are derived from economic theory and they are employed to model 
households' consumption behavior and changes in welfare. The system 
represents consumption decisions as a system of equations which de
-
pend on prices, consumption budgets, and both observed and un-
observed household characteristics. Demand systems have been used to 
study households' energy use and carbon emissions (see Baker et al., 
1989
; Creedy and Sleeman, 2006; Labandeira et al., 2006; Pashardes 
et al., 2014; Sommer and Kratena, 2017; Tovar-Reaños and Wölfing, 
2018). However, to the best of my knowledge, this is the first study that 
investigates the distributional effects of floods and flood policies using a 
demand system approach. 
The research approach used here consists of two steps, as does the 
methodology: First, the expected annual flood damage is estimated for 
various household types. In order to compute the metric for the eco
-
nomic damage, the following probabilities are estimated: of living in a 
floodplain, of having insurance, of investing in mitigation measures. In 
order to estimate these probabilities, the Eval-MAP dataset is used, a 
unique household panel survey covering more than 10,000 households 
in Germany over a period of four years. Second, the exact affine Stone 
index (EASI) developed by Lewbel and Pendakur (2009) is estimated. 
The EASI demand system is employed because it allows for high flex-
ibility when modeling the relationship between household budget and 
commodity demand. Given that changes in income and changes in the 
price of energy commodities will be simulated, the demand system suits 
the research purposes as it allows for simulating welfare changes due to 
changes in income and commodity prices. The waves from 1993 to 
2013 from the German Einkommens- und Verbrauchsstichprobe (EVS) 
are used. The proposed model in the study takes into consideration 
changes in income and expenditure levels on different commodities and 
their welfare implications. It also identifies which groups face the larger 
burden after the flood. 
Results show the first empirical evidence that economic damage 
after floods in Germany increases inequality. When these losses are 
translated into the cost that families will face when adjusting their 
consumption patterns due to the economic losses, it is found that fa-
milies with children and households with a head at retirement age are 
the most affected. Raising revenues at the level of the economic da-
mage, income, real estate and carbon taxes can reduce inequality. When 
revenues from energy taxes are jointly used to compensate households 
for the regressive nature of the tax and to incentivise investment in risk 
reduction, further reductions in inequality can be achieved. The paper 
proceeds as follows. Section 2 depicts the data sources used in this ar-
ticle. Section 3 presents the methodological approach. Section 3.6 ex-
plains the simulated scenarios. The results are shown in Section 4
.  
Sections 5 and 6 provides a policy discussion and concludes. 
2. Data sources and descriptive statistics 
The German Einkommens- und Verbrauchsstichprobe (survey of 
income and expenditure, EVS) is used to estimate the EASI demand 
system and carry out the simulation. It has been recently used to esti-
mate an EASI demand system by Pothen and Tovar-Reaños (2018)
. The 
survey is carried out every five years and provides information on ex-
penditures across consumer commodities and other socio-economic 
variables. Coherence in the survey is achieved by interviews at the 
beginning and at the end of the survey. Moreover, the survey has a short 
panel structure in which households are followed across a short period 
of time. This provides consistency of the reported data across time. The 
waves 1998, 2003, 2008 and 2013 are used for the econometric esti
-
mation. This provides a sample of 166,600 observations. The wave 
2013 is used for the microsimulation with a sample size of 38,634 
households. 
Household types are distinguished according to two criteria. First, 
four expenditure quartiles are differentiated. Second, households with 
different socioeconomic characteristics are distinguished. Lewbel 
(1989)
's methodology is used to obtain household-specific prices by 
combining the micro data with prices reported by the German Statis-
tical Office. 
For the demand system, six consumption purposes i
: food, housing, 
energy, transport, education and others are analyzed. Table A.1 in the 
Appendix displays the descriptive statistics for expenditure and the 
price variables used in the estimation. The aggregation of commodities 
used in the demand system resembles Pothen and Tovar-Reaños (2018). 
Demand of durable commodities is not included in this analysis because 
they are not regularly consumed and their demand follows an invest
-
ment behavior.

3. Models and methodology 
3.1. Model overview 
Here an overview of the models is provided. In order to measure 
changes in income and expenditure after floods, data on household 
income, expenditure, risk of suffering flood damage, probability of 
being insured against floods, probability of living in a ground floor or 
basement and data on the damage cost is needed. In addition, a theory- 
consistent model is also used to measure changes in welfare at the 
household and aggregate levels resulting from floods and flood policies. 
To do so, probabilities of experiencing a flood and other flood-related 
variables are estimated using the Eval-MAP survey, which collects in-
formation on these matters and other socioeconomic variables. 
Information on average damage from the German insurance association 
(GDV, 2017) is used to estimate the expected flood damage at house-
hold level. These probabilities are then imputed to data on expenditure 
and income (the so-called Einkommens-und Verbrauchsstichprobe, 
EVS). The EVS is used to estimate a structural model that allows us to 
measure changes in welfare and inequality. Fig. 1 provides a descrip-
tion of the process followed in this research. 
3.2. Expected flood damage at the household level 
As mentioned above, a crucial part of the modeling exercise is the 
estimation of an expected value of the annual flood damage for the 
households in the EVS dataset. In this section, a description of the data 
sources, methods, and assumptions on which these estimates are based 
is provided. The total flood damage is divided into direct damage oc-
curring at the household level (home and contents), and indirect da-
mage affecting households through the destruction of public infra-
structure, such as transport, electricity and communication grids. First, 
let us turn to the direct damage affecting homes and contents. Given the 
lack of detailed damage data on the household level, the general 
strategy relies on (a) aggregate damage statistics of the German 
insurance association (GDV, 2017) and (b) the distribution of these 
aggregates on household types based on relevant household para-
meters, including the following:  
• estimated flood risk  
• estimated probability of using ground floor or basement  
• estimated probability of the implementation of flood mitigation 
measures  
• estimated expected insurance coverage, separately for home and 
contents  
• reported size of the dwelling 
The estimated values are predictions based on probit regressions, 
which are presented in more detail in the appendix. In general, pre-
dicted probabilities for households (e.g. of living in a floodplain) are 
computed using the Eval-MAP dataset. The Eval-MAP dataset is a panel 
survey covering a largely representative sample of households in 
Germany, focusing inter alia on flood protection, flood insurance, and 
related variables at the household level. It covers more than 10,000 
households in four annual waves over the period 2012–2015 (for more 
information on the Eval-MAP data, refer to Osberghaus (2017) and  
Osberghaus and Philippi (2015)). For estimating flood risk, the survey 
data is enriched with objective, GIS-based flood risk data on the exact 
location of the respondent (BfG, 2015). In the regressions, predictors 
which are readily available in the EVS dataset are also used. These are 
income quartiles, household size, employment status, homeownership, 
type of housing, urbanization category of the place of residence, and 
age of the household head (see Table A.2 in the appendix). Next, pre-
dicted probabilities (e.g. of living in a floodplain) are imputed for the 
households in the EVS dataset, using the regression results from the 
Eval-MAP data. Table A.3 in the Appendix explains in more detail how 
these and other parameters are used to derive an estimate of the annual 
expected flood damage, and what the underlying assumptions are. The 
variables and estimates to compute expected flood damage are dis-
played in Table A.4. 
Besides the damage at the household level, floods in the past have 
caused severe damage to public infrastructure. In the case of the most 
recent major flood in Germany in June 2013, damage to public infra
-
structure was around five times as high as damage to households 
(Bundesregierung, 2016). Obviously, infrastructure damage increases 
the financial burden the government has to carry after a flood and for 
this reason this type of damage is included when calculating the fiscal 
consequences for the households. Different household types might, 
however, also be differently affected by infrastructure failure. Hence, 
this damage should not be ignored if we are interested in the flood 
effects on welfare and inequality. When adding infrastructure damage 
to the estimation of expected annual flood damage, the underlying as
-
sumption is that the more persons living in a household, the more it is 
affected by infrastructure failure. Moreover, households residing in 
floodplains should be more affected.

3.3. The Exact Affine Stone Index (EASI) implicit Marshallian demand 
system 
The exact affine Stone index was recently proposed by (EASI;  
Lewbel and Pendakur, 2009; Pendakur, 2009). The methodology has 
been applied by Pothen and Tovar-Reaños (2018) to estimate own, 
cross and expenditure elasticities. The fraction of the household budget 
that is spent on specific commodities is the dependent variable in each 
equation in the system. It is a function of commodity prices, total ex
-
penditure and other socioeconomic variables. The EASI demand system 
approximates an expenditure function from which one can obtain 
Marshallian demand functions. The EASI extends its predecessors that 
assume linear Engel curves (
Deaton and Muellbauer, 1980) or quadratic 
curves (Banks et al., 1997). The EASI system allows for any shape be-
tween expenditure and expenditure budget (the Engels curves). The 
iterated three-stage least squares (3SLS) estimator is used when esti-
mating the parameters from the demand system. Once the parameters 
are estimated, one can estimate an expenditure function and compute 
changes in welfare.

4. Results 
Table 3 summarises the descriptive statistics for the expected values 
with and without infrastructure damage. The absolute values seem to 
be relatively small, given the annual median income. In reality, most 
households will experience many years without any damage, but when 
flooded, the damages will be much higher than the numbers presented 
here. Also note that due to climate change, flood damage is expected to 
rise in central Europe during the 21st century (Alfieri et al., 2015). 
Finally, note that the main contribution of this paper is analysing the 
directions of distributional effects. While the estimated flood damage 
may be disputable in quantitative terms, it is argued that the relative 
distribution on household types is well reasoned and justified by de-
tailed and unique household level data. 
As explained in Section 3
, an expected value of annual flood damage 
for different household types is estimated. To the best of my knowledge, 
this is the first attempt to derive such estimates on the national scale, 
taking account of detailed household parameters such as insurance and 
mitigation probabilities. Hence, the resulting correlations of income, 
expected annual flood damage, and relevant parameters at household 
level provide interesting preliminary insights into the distributional 
implications of flooding in Germany (see Table 4). 
The correlations suggest that high income households have a higher 
probability of being insured, living in safe areas, and having im-
plemented physical or behavioural flood mitigation measures than 
poorer households. In other words, their adaptive capacity is relatively 
high, which contributes to lower expected flood damage. At the same 
time, they tend to be more sensitive to flooding as they are more likely 
to own a home, and have larger dwellings. The estimates of expected 
annual flood damage in absolute term, show that the latter effect of 
higher sensitivity seems to dominate the former effect of higher adap
-
tive capacity. In short, richer households face higher absolute values of 
expected annual flood damage. There is a negative correlation between 
EAFD and flood risk that can mistakenly lead one to conclude that 
households facing high risk will face low damages. Dwelling damage is 
the main component of the estimated EAFD. When households have a 
high probability of being home insured, the damage will reduce. Note 
that high flood risk is correlated with the probability of being home 
insured. Nevertheless, if we consider the relative importance of ex-
pected flood damage for the households by formulating damages as

I would like to add the caveat that while the estimations of expected 
damage take account of the likelihood of insurance coverage, they 
neglect the cost of insurance paid by the households that are insured. 
Given the higher probability of being insured and larger dwelling sizes 
of high income households, their insurance premiums are probably 
higher as well. At the same time, richer households tend to be located in 
safer areas, which is decisive for the premium. The cost of insurance at 
this point is excluded, because expenditure on insurance (including 
flood insurance) is already included in the expenditure data of the EVS 
dataset, and calculating an expected value would imply double 
counting of these expenditures in the welfare analysis. In addition, note 
that the expected damage is not the damage suffered and reported in a 
single random draw of the range of flood losses, and it cannot capture 
the entire risk spectrum faced as reported data. 
In order to raise financial resources that account for 1% of the 
household income in the analyzed survey, it is estimated that income 
taxes paid by the household have to increase by 3%. If the source of 
resources come from real estate taxes, they have to increase by 84%. 
Unlike income and wealth taxes that tend to create a larger burden for 
wealthy households, carbon taxes are regressive. These regressive ef-
fects can be overcome by allocating the additional income revenues. In 
order to create enough resources to cover the cost of the damage, the 
price of energy commodities need to increase by 3%. It is estimated that 
this tax will generate resources equal to 2.5% of the income in the 
analyzed sample. These resources will be enough for the relief and 
additional revenues to be allocated and compensate for possible re-
gressive effects. 
4.1. Household welfare changes 
The parameters from the demand system are displayed in Table A.5 
in the Appendix. Estimated own price and expenditure elasticities are 
displayed in Table 5. These estimates resemble the work of Pothen and 
Tovar-Reaños (2018) and Nikodinoska and Schröder (2016) who also 
used German data and a demand system approach. In line with their 
findings, own price elasticity of food, housing, energy and transport are 
found to be inelastic, that is, the absolute value of these estimates is less 
than one. As to the expenditure elasticities, these commodities are 
found to be necessary goods given that the value of the estimates is less 
than one. This implies that increases in prices of these commodities via 
taxes will impose a larger burden to poorer households. With the esti-
mated parameters from the demand system, metrics for equivalent 
variation are computed using expression (5). These estimates are dis-
played in Table 6 and the values are relative to household income. 
Scenario 1 for the flood incidence shows that households face welfare 
losses, which disproportionately affect low income households. In this 
scenario, welfare losses for families with children and households with 
a head at retirement age are larger than the sample mean of this metric. 
Note that the metrics are statistically different from the sample mean. In 
the second scenario the government increases income taxes to fund the 
relief. One can see that the welfare losses decrease with respect to the 
previous scenario. Note that this policy is also progressive because the 
more affluent households face the largest welfare losses. When real 
estate taxes are used instead of income taxes, tenants have a reduction 
in welfare losses but households with a head in retirement age face an 
increase in their losses. This is explained by the fact that this last group

4.2. Inequality and social welfare changes 
Table 8 shows the Atkinson inequality index (AI), equivalent income 
(EI), social welfare (SW), and the changes in these metrics. These values 
are provided quarterly. The changes are related to the base scenario of 
no flood, thus without changes in income and commodity prices. One 
can see that the expected economic damage after the flood can cause an 
increase in inequality. In this scenario, an income shock after a flood 
decreases equivalent income and social welfare. 
Research on changes in inequality due to floods is narrow.  
Yamamura (2015) estimates that floods can increase income inequality, 
measured by a Gini coefficient, by 1%. They used a panel of emerging 
economies and econometrics for panel data to obtain this metric. The 
estimate of 0.14% found here can be a low bound estimate. However, 
insurance coverage and existing infrastructure to mitigate the effects in 
flood in Germany can also explain the size of the estimated value. Note 
that general equilibrium effects regarding inequality can be larger than 
the ones found with our partial equilibrium model. For instance, Leiter

transfers the burden from low income to high income households. In 
these scenarios, there is also a reduction in social welfare, but the loss is 
inferior to the first scenario. Note that the use of real estate taxes has 
the largest reduction in inequality. As to the increases in energy taxes, 
inequality also decreases. There is a decrease in social welfare because 
there is a drop in equivalent income (EI). In accordance with the result 
presented in the previous section, Table 8 also shows that targeting 
improvements in sustainable consumption, inequality, and rewarding 
risk reduction can be achieved jointly. Regarding the difference be

Chancel and Piketty, 2015

suggested by Jacobs and van der Ploeg (2019), could reduce inequality, 
pollution damages and pressure on public finances.

equivalent income. Consequently, households over the distribution of 
income must still pay for the damage to be recouped. 
5. Policy discussion 
The fiscal impact of the flood damage can be sizable when extreme 
events are expected to become more frequent. This can cause financial 
deviations leading to a public debt. This calls for the creation of a fiscal 
buffer to cover the cost of the implementation of strategies that en-
courage households to insure themselves, and government investment 
in infrastructure to reduce the risk. Clear rules to manage the additional 
resources have been suggested by the IMF (see Cevik and Huang, 2018
). 
The suggested rules cover transparent use of the resources, and po-
tential investment of the additional resources on financial instruments 
for periods of time free of floods. Providing government transfers based 
on risk-reduction behavior can create the incentives to increase the 
number of insured households and reduce the damage cost faced by 
insurers and governments. The design of policy instruments, where 
sustainable consumption and the promotion of investment are jointly 
considered, can increase the participation of the financial sector in the 
transition towards reducing the exposure to flood risk, and affordability 
of insurance premiums. Public–Private Partnerships (PPPs) are existing 
examples of cooperation between governments and insurers. It is a 
scheme where governments and financial companies jointly bear re
-
sponsibilities and efficient risk sharing when increasing the number of 
insured households (see Castellar et al., 2017 ). Mechanisms for the 
cooperation between governments and insurers to tackle climate 
change, risk management, and adaptation has been suggested by  
Dlugolecki (2008). The author suggests that multi-year contracts are an 
instrument to encourage customers to adopt flood-proofing measures 
and access to cheap loans that help towards such mitigation invest-
ments. In addition, Surminski and Thieken (2017) provide examples of 
existing cooperation between the German government and insurance 
companies. Insurance companies have driven the development of a 
hazard zoning system that creates an awareness of the risk faced by 
households. Regarding affordability of the insurance premium, invest-
ment in flood protection could reduce the flood damage and might 
make insurance premiums more affordable. Kousky and Kunreuther 
(2014) suggest providing subsidies to reduce the flood risk via vouchers 
to low income households. Gruber (2008) suggests using financial 
measures such as captive structure and catastrophe bonds, which could 
make insurance premiums and the burden of compensation against 
extreme events more affordable. Captive structures allow individuals or 
institutions acting as insurers to, in some cases be sponsored by other 
entities. Cat bonds allow insurance companies to sell bonds to investors. 
In the absence of extreme events, the insurance company pays a coupon 
to the investor. However, if the event occurs during the duration of the 
bond (normally during 3 years), the insurance company can use the 
principal from the bonds to finance the claims. 
McAneney et al. (2016) acknowledge the existence of a trade-off 
between an insurance system with risk-reflective premiums and af
-
fordability. They argue that in US, France, New Zealand, Spain and the 
United Kingdom, the intervention of the government by sponsoring 
insurance pools or providing financial aid after the disaster cannot be 
financially sustainable. The problem is that this mechanism does not 
create incentives to reduce the risk faced by households. The scenarios 
2–4, where there is a financial relief, are close to this mechanism. This 
mechanism would remove the entire incentive structure to purchase 
private flood insurance. The scenarios 5–6 proposed a mechanism that 
encourages risk reduction behavior by offering transfers in proportion 
to the investment in insurance and mitigation. 
Poussin et al. (2013) proposes to improve the effectiveness of risk 
prevention plans and their implementation in France. They recommend 
improving the monitoring of the adoption of mitigation measures, and 
the possibility to differentiate premiums and deductibles according to 
flood risk. This last recommendation to reduce flood risk is close to the 
simulated scenarios 5 and 6. 
6. Conclusion 
The first simulation of the distributional effects of floods and flood 
policies in Germany is undertaken. A welfare approach consistent with 
economic theory is used to measure changes in welfare at the household 
and aggregate levels of the expected-flood damage. It is found that the 
expected flood damage will disproportionally hurt low income house
-
holds. This particularly applies to families with dependent children and 
households with a head at retirement age. Investing in infrastructure or 
offering subsidies to cover insurance for low income households will 
require additional resources. Raising resources that are comparable to 
the flood damage yields lower levels of inequality. This is the case of
