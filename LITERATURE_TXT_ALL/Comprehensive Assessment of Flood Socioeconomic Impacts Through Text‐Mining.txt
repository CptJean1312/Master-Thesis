Abstract In July 2021, Germany experienced its costliest riverine floods in history, with over 189 fatalities
and a staggering €33 billion in damages. Following this event, news outlets widely disseminated information on
the flood's aftermath. Here, we demonstrate how newspaper data can be instrumental in the assessment of flood
socioeconomic impacts often overlooked by conventional methods. Using natural language processing tools on
26,113 newspaper articles, we estimate the cascading impacts of the 2021 flood on various sectors and critical
infrastructure, including water contamination, mental health, and tourism. Our results revealed severe and
lasting impacts in the Ahr Valley, even months after the event. At the same time, we identified smaller‐scale yet
widespread impacts across Germany, which are typically overlooked by existing impact databases. Our
approach advances current research by systematically examining indirect and intangible flood consequences
over large areas. This underscores the value of leveraging complementary text data to provide a more
comprehensive picture of flood impacts.
1. Introduction
Flood disasters are predicted to become more frequent in many regions due to climate, land use, and population
demographic changes (Merz et al., 2021 ; Seneviratne et al., 2021 ), as well as the increasing interconnectedness of
coupled human‐water systems (Fekete et al., 2021 ; Yoon et al., 2021 ). Despite this expected increase, our
knowledge of flood's direct and indirect impacts remains fragmented. Existing studies based on damage functions
(Carrera et al., 2015 ; Merz et al., 2010 ) and surveys (Kreibich et al., 2010 ) often provide only a partial picture of
flood consequences. A major challenge of these studies is that they often focus on direct impacts and analyze only
one impact type at a time. Examples include the assessment of impacts on critical infrastructure (e.g., Qiang
et al., 2020 ), agriculture (e.g., Chen et al., 2019 ; Rahman & Di, 2020 ; Tapia‐Silva et al., 2011 ), buildings (e.g.,
Gerl et al., 2014 ; Serpico et al., 2012 ), and fatalities (e.g., Papagiannaki et al., 2022 ). Moreover, detailed impact
estimation studies often have a limited geographic scope. For instance, assessments of mental health distress due
to floods, while detailed, are often restricted to single neighborhoods or districts (e.g., Francesca & Fie-
drich, 2024 ; Zenker et al., 2024 ). These limitations are linked to difficulties in obtaining reliable estimates for
diverse impact types simultaneously due to the time‐or data‐intensive nature of conventional impact assessment
methods (Carrera et al., 2015 ; Kreibich et al., 2010 ; Merz et al., 2010 ).
An additional gap in impact assessments refers to the emphasis on severe and large‐scale floods. Nevertheless,
99.7% of all disasters worldwide between 1990 and 2013 were smaller events involving fewer than 30 deaths or
less than 5,000 houses destroyed (UNISDR, 2015 ). Thousands of smaller‐scale floods thus go unrecorded
because they do not generate sufficiently high impacts at the national or international levels. However, they cause
a continuous stream of local losses (UNDRR, 2021 ). As a result, flood impacts have historically been under-
estimated (Sanders et al., 2022 ).
The scarcity of data sets covering both direct and indirect impacts, as well as low and high‐impact events,
constrains our ability to understand the far‐reaching consequences of floods (de Brito et al., 2024 ). Hence,
comprehensive assessments of how floods affect society can better support effective adaptation. Impact data
covering diverse sectors (e.g., tourism, agriculture, infrastructure), including tangible and intangible losses, are
needed to improve our understanding of impact dynamics (Kellermann et al., 2020 ) and serve as validation data
for impact‐based forecasting systems (Mitheu et al., 2023 ). Such assessments can equip decision‐makers with a
deeper understanding of all the potential flood consequences. Based on that, they can identify areas with severe

As researchers face the need for comprehensive methods to produce impact data sets, the use of text data for
extracting information on natural hazard consequences is flourishing (e.g., Alencar et al., 2024; de Brito
et al., 2020; Franceschini et al., 2022; Hao & Wang, 2020; Li et al., 2021; Liu & Jensen, 2018). Texts offer a rich
yet untapped complement to the more structured data types traditionally used in research (Gentzkow et al., 2019;
Nunes Carvalho et al., 2024). Texts are essential to our lives—they are how historical events are recorded, and
citizens' concerns are documented. Within this context, computational text analysis offers a unique opportunity to
automatically extract information on flood impacts stored in vast amounts of text. While natural language pro-
cessing (NLP) tools have proven effective in detecting drought impacts from newspaper articles (e.g., Alencar
et al., 2024; Sodoge et al., 2023), their applicability to flood events is yet to be tested. Floods, characterized by
their brief and localized nature, present distinct challenges compared to droughts' long‐lasting and widespread
nature. Therefore, leveraging newspapers for flood impact analysis presents unique challenges.
Here, we use newspaper texts to infer the socioeconomic impacts of the July 2021 floods in Germany, particularly
in the Ahr River region but also in the South and East of the country. The disaster in the Ahr River was Germany's
most severe flood event ever recorded, with unprecedented impacts and unmatched precipitation values (Jun-
ghänel et al., 2021; Kreienkamp et al., 2021; Kuhlicke et al., 2024). It affected more than 40,000 people (Zeit
Online, 2021), causing over 189 direct fatalities and at least €33 billion in damage (Dietze et al., 2022; Fekete &
Sandholz, 2021; Kron et al., 2022; Thieken et al., 2022). The main flood event occurred between the 12th and 19th
of July, with an estimated return period exceeding 500 years in the Ahr River (Kreienkamp et al., 2021). Given its
widespread consequences and extensive media coverage, we view this disaster as a test case for investigating
whether newspaper articles can provide reliable data on flood impacts for both large and small‐scale events. By
examining this extreme case, we can verify whether minor floods happening far from the catastrophe in the Ahr
River area are potentially “ignored” by the media or if it is possible to capture them based on local and regional
news outlets.
Our study provides a comprehensive overview of the multifaceted effects of the 2021 floods in Germany,
demonstrating that newspaper articles constitute a rich data source to assess flood socioeconomic impacts for both
large and small‐scale events. Using NLP tools on a newspaper corpus, we provide empirical insights into the
impacts of the 2021 floods in Germany and their varying spatio‐temporal magnitude. We examine indirect and
intangible impacts often neglected by conventional methods. As such, our NLP‐based model shifts the focus away
from damages to buildings and fatalities to encompass 20 distinct socioeconomic impact classes (e.g., mental
health, water contamination). We evaluate our text‐based estimates of impacts against a series of independently
collected data to examine if the news media reporting is linked to observed water levels and to how the gov-
ernment perceived the flood consequences (i.e., by declaring a status of emergency).
2. Methods
2.1. Newspaper Data Collection and Pre‐Processing
We used NLP tools to determine text patterns that predict the socioeconomic impacts of floods on multiple sectors
based on newspaper data from a news aggregator database (www.wiso‐net.de). All the 250 printed and online
news outlets within the Wiso newspaper database were considered. These comprise 144 local, 63 regional, and 43
national outlets (see Table S1 in Supporting Information S1 for a list of all newspapers included). To identify
relevant articles published in these 250 news outlets between 01.07.2021 and 31.12.2021, we searched the title
and subtitle of the articles using various flood‐related keywords in German (Box 1). We tested different sets of
synonyms for “flood” and manually examined the outcomes to verify whether all relevant articles were captured.
Following the approach by Hase et al. (2021), we optimized our search to prioritize recall, that is, including a few
irrelevant articles for the sake of completeness. To filter out news mentioning floods in other countries, we only
considered articles with a country tag related to Germany. The accuracy of our search was verified by randomly
evaluating a sample of 100 articles. By manually inspecting these, we found that all were related to floods, with
only four referencing floods outside Germany. These irrelevant articles were later excluded by our location
extraction approach (see Section 2.5). Based on this search, we retrieved 26,113 articles covering local, regional,
and national news. An overview of the procedure used to create the multi‐sector impact database is provided

To ensure that our corpus consisted of unique articles, we computed Jaccard similarity scores for all article pairs.
The Jaccard score measures the proportion of shared word sequences between two texts (Mullen, 2016 ). After
analyzing the articles' similarity histogram and manually reading 30 article pairs, we removed duplicate articles
with a similarity score greater than 0.75, where 1 indicates full similarity. This process left 14,888 unique articles
for further analysis (Figure 1 ), with an average length of 467 words.
We cleaned the raw text data by decomposing each newspaper article into single words and converting them to
their root form through lemmatizetion. Furthermore, we removed special characters, stopwords (e.g., pronouns,
articles), words that consisted of less than two characters, and words used less than 10 times in the entire corpus.
2.2. Impact Typology
To classify the articles according to specific impacts, we developed a multi‐sector flood socioeconomic impacts
typology using both inductive and deductive approaches. For the inductive one, we built a topic model to explore
the text corpora and identify key topics directly from the data. By doing so, we wanted to avoid missing relevant
impacts that were not considered in previous studies. Topic modeling is an NLP technique that allows for
discovering latent topics in the text without requiring predefined classes (Salley et al., 2024 ). In this study, we
used the Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) algorithm. LDA assumes that each document is a mixture of topics
and each topic is a distribution of words. Topic coherence was used to determine the optimal number of topics, as
it offers a quantitative assessment of the meaningfulness and organization of topics within a corpus. After testing
topic coherence across different numbers of topics (i.e., from 10 to 120), we set the number of topics to 65. The

While informative, the LDA results overlooked particular impact types (e.g., tourism, water contamination,

resented classes (Thielmann et al., 2023 ). To address this limitation, we also followed a knowledge‐driven
deductive approach, leveraging the co‐authors’ expertise in flood impact assessment and previous research on
the flood consequences (e.g., Hammond et al., 2015 ; Koks et al., 2022 ; Papagiannaki et al., 2022 ). As a result, we
developed a comprehensive typology of 20 multi‐sector flood socioeconomic impact classes (Table S3 in Sup-
porting Information S1 ). It incorporates key impact classes commonly included in flood impact assessments (e.g.,
economic losses, fatalities, and damage to buildings) while exploring additional classes rarely examined (e.g.,
flood impacts on tourism and mental health).
2.3. Impact Data Annotation
After defining this typology, we manually read and annotated a random subset of the unique articles with these 20
specific impact categories. In order to build accurate machine learning classification models, we aimed for at least
50 positive labels (e.g., articles reporting an impact) for each impact class. Since some articles mentioned multiple
impact classes, we annotated only 640 articles, totaling 1,582 positive impact labels (for the number of labeled
data per impact class, see Table S5 in Supporting Information S1 ).
The articles were hand‐coded by the first author with the support of a student assistant, following the principles of
qualitative content analysis (de Brito et al., 2021 ; Schreier, 2012 ). Both annotators initially coded the same set of
50 articles to ensure consistency. This process was then repeated with a set of 100 documents. By this stage, we
achieved an average agreement of 96% (F1‐score), indicating high reliability in our annotations. However, a
higher number of annotators is preferred when feasible. The resulting annotated impact data set formed the basis
for evaluating all impact classification models.
2.4. Classification Models for the Assessment of Floods' Socioeconomic Impacts
We developed five multilabel classification models to assess flood's socioeconomic impacts from the unique
articles ( n = 14,888). The term “multilabel” implies that each article may contain multiple impact classes. To
construct these models, we considered both dictionary‐based as well as machine‐learning approaches.
To develop the dictionary‐based algorithm, we relied on domain knowledge and dictionaries from previous
studies (e.g., Alderman et al., 2012 ; de Brito et al., 2020 ; Saulnier et al., 2017 ; Yari et al., 2020 ). We also analyzed
word frequencies, word co‐occurrences, and topic modeling results (Table S2 in Supporting Information S1 ). For
each impact class, we considered a series of seed keywords (e.g., energy, transportation) and used the word2vec
algorithm (Jan Wijffels et al., 2023 ) to identify similar terms. This approach measures the semantic similarity
between words and helped us to expand the dictionary. All co‐authors, who have in‐depth expertise in flood
impact assessment, contributed to selecting relevant keywords. The resulting dictionary (Table S4 in Supporting
Information S1 ) was used to identify sentences where these keywords occurred and tag them as related to a given
impact class.
In addition to the dictionary‐based approach, we also tested four machine learning models using the Python scikit‐
multilearn library (Szymánski & Kajdanowicz, 2019 ): (a) logistic regression, (b) support vector machine, (c)
random forest, and (d) XGboost. A description of each of these models is provided in the scikit‐multilearn library
user guide (Szyma & Kajdanowicz, 2016 ). To train and test the models, we randomly split the annotated data set
( n = 640) into 70% for training and 30% for testing.
We evaluated the predictions of all five models against the manually annotated data set ( n = 640 articles). To do

2.5. Location Extraction
Extracting location information from text is a challenging and extensively studied NLP task involving identifying
and disambiguating place names (Li et al., 2024 ; Middleton et al., 2014 ; Zheng et al., 2018 ). Usually, studies
attempt to identify all locations mentioned in a text (e.g., van Daalen et al., 2024 ). However, since our aim here
was to assess the impacts of the 2021 floods, not all locations mentioned are relevant to the flood impacts. As
such, additional steps were needed to ensure the location extraction results reflected the impacts locations.
To extract the impact‐related locations in each article, we followed the three‐step method proposed by Sodoge
et al. ( 2023 ) (see Figure S2 in Supporting Information S1 ). First, we used the Google BERT named entity
recognition algorithm (Devlin et al., 2018 ) to identify all location names mentioned in the text. These include
names of regions, cities, neighbourhoods, streets, and rivers. This procedure was done at the word or sequences of
words level. This step resulted in a list with all locations mentioned in each article.
Next, we matched the identified locations with the GeoNames gazetteer (GeoNames, 2022 ) to obtain their
corresponding geographical coordinates. Given that the names of these locations are not unambiguous in the
GeoNames data set, we excluded locations whose name appeared more than 5 times in the gazetteer data. This
step was necessary to eliminate locations that cannot be unambiguously identified. For example, hundreds of
streets in Germany are named “ Hauptstraße ” (“main street” in English). This measure affected only detailed
location names (e.g., neighborhoods and streets) as district names are unique according to the European Union
Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics (NUTS).
Finally, to remove locations that were mentioned but not affected by floods (e.g., location of the newspaper
headquarters), we excluded those geographically separated from the majority of identified locations. For instance,
newspaper headquarters tend to be mentioned once at the beginning of the articles while they are not related to the
impacts described in the text. To this end, an agglomerative hierarchical clustering algorithm known as Wards's
linkage method was used (Murtagh & Contreras, 2012 ). This technique begins by treating each identified location
as its own cluster, then progressively merges the two closest clusters at each step based on their geographic
proximity. The largest resulting cluster was assumed to represent the area directly impacted by the flood, while
any distant or sparsely connected locations were treated as outliers and excluded from further analysis. For
instance, if an article mentioned three districts, including two that were closely located (districts A and B) and one
far away (district C), the clustering algorithm would only map the two closely located districts due to their high
location tag density. Different from the approach by Sodoge et al. ( 2023 ), we considered a lower cutoff value (ξ)
for the hierarchical clustering which describes the pre‐specified maximum distance between the two closest
clusters. For the selected cutoff value, the algorithms results in clusters with a maximum diameter of approxi-
mately 75 km. This value was selected as it resulted in highest accuracy of identifying the true impact location.
This was needed as floods are much more localized than droughts. We then aggregated the obtained data by their
NUTS level 3, which refers to the most detailed NUTS classification (i.e., district scale).
The three‐step location extraction method was thoroughly evaluated in previous research (Alencar et al., 2024 ;
Sodoge et al., 2022 , 2023 ). Here, we tested its performance using a random sample of 50 newspaper articles. By
manually reading them, we verified whether the extracted impact‐related locations ( n = 236) were mentioned in
the text. We focused on the locations identified after applying the hierarchical clustering and removing outliers
(e.g., newspaper headquarters). We found that all impact‐related locations extracted (100%) were indeed
mentioned in the text. However, not all extracted locations referred to the actual sites of flood consequences. As

2.6. Flood Impact Statements (FIS) Spatiotemporal Database
To identify the time of the impact occurrence, we considered the time stamps of the newspapers. The identified
impacts with a corresponding location were integrated into a final spatial‐temporal data set of flood impact
statements (FIS). A FIS consists of a sentence that describes a flood impact classified into 20 different classes
(Table S2 in Supporting Information S1) and locates it at the NUTS 3 scale (i.e., district level). One article can
contain multiple FIS of different classes. However, an article can only contain one FIS of the same class, even
when multiple sentences refer to the same impact.
2.7. Empirical Evaluation of the FIS Data Set
To verify to which extent the FIS data set corresponds to observed reality, we evaluated it against: (a) the flood
emergency levels issued by the German Civil Defense, (b) the number of flood fatalities recorded in each district,
(c) the maximum flood water levels observed in each district in July 2021, and (d) a flood damage model
(Table 1).
Standard non‐parametric tests were used to measure the strength and direction of the relationship between the FIS
impact data set and these independent variables. We computed Spearman rho coefficients between the data in
Table 1 and the FIS impact data set. Furthermore, the Kruskal‐Wallis test was performed to test the differences in
distribution between the number of FIS and categorical variables (i.e., flood emergency levels and maximum
flood water level observed).
To verify biases introduced by the media reporting as more news are produced in heavily populated cities, we
contrasted the FIS magnitude with the population size in each district (Figure S6 in Supporting Information S1).

3. Results and Discussion
3.1. Consequences of the 2021 German Floods on Multiple Sectors According to Newspaper Articles
Following the devastating floods in Germany in July 2021 (Junghänel et al., 2021 ; Kreienkamp et al., 2021 ), both
printed and online media provided extensive coverage of their aftermath. Over 26,000 flood‐related news were
published between July and December 2021 in Germany, which became the primary data source for this study.
To uncover and categorize the consequences of this disaster as reported by newspapers, we designed five different
NLP‐based classification models. After evaluating their performance against a manually annodated data set, we
found that the dictionary‐based approach was the most accurate, with an average accuracy of 98% and an F1‐score
of 91% (Figure S3 and Table S5 in Supporting Information S1 ). As such, we used it throughout this study to assign
one or more impact classes to each article. By applying the dictionary‐based model to the newspaper corpus, we
identified 20,052 FIS. Each FIS consists of a sentence that describes and locates a flood impact at the district level.
An article can only contain one FIS of the same class, even when multiple sentences refer to the same impact.
Each article describes an average of 1.34 FIS (mode = 0, standard deviation 1.58).
Our analyses revealed that the floods had far‐reaching consequences on multiple sectors, affecting critical
infrastructure, individuals, buildings, and different economic sectors (Figure 2 ). Numerous critical infrastructures
were disrupted, including roads, wastewater treatment plants, water supply, mobile phone services, and elec-
tricity, gas, and heating systems. Media reports on “transport and mobility” FIS were particularly prevalent, being
mentioned in 3,038 articles. These describe disruptions to roads, railways, and the collapse of bridges. Of the 112
bridges located in the flooded area of the Ahr valley, 62 were destroyed (Schäfer et al., 2021 ). Until July 2022,
none of the 18 bridges on the Ahr river were functional, and temporary crossings were used instead (The
Guardian, 2022 ). Furthermore, major access routes, 600 km of railway, and 50 railway bridges were damaged,
causing significant disruptions to transportation (Schäfer et al., 2021 ).
A significant number of “electricity, gas and heating” FIS ( n = 1,580) were reported. More than 200,000
households were left without energy–some for months (Reuters, 2021 ). The contamination of rivers and drinking
water due to floating and bursting oil tanks (Bosseler et al., 2021 ; Koks et al., 2021 ) was also a major concern, as

3.2. Magnitude of Flood Socioeconomic Impacts
To estimate the magnitude of the socioeconomic impacts, we aggregated the counts of FIS over time (01 July to
31 December 2021). This choice was motivated by the observation that disasters with widespread effects typically
attract heightened media coverage, as shown by previous studies (Kryvasheyeu et al., 2016 ; Li et al., 2018 ;
Sodoge et al., 2023 ). The FIS magnitude was then compared with a series of independent data to investigate the
reliability of this assumption.
Overall, we found that trends in the socioeconomic impacts of floods can be robustly inferred from the FIS
magnitude derived based on news coverage. Figure 3a shows that the impact magnitude increases with the
proximity to stream gauges with recorded extreme floods (Figure 3b ). Districts with “extreme,” and “major” flood
levels have significantly more FIS in July (Kruskal‐Wallis test, p < 0.0001) (Figure 4a ). The districts with the
highest FIS magnitude are located along the Ahr river, where water levels reached their highest values since

3.3. Dynamics of FIS Across Time, Regions, and Sectors
When analyzing the time transect of the FIS (Figure 6a ), we find that their peak was on 24 July, days after the main
floods on 12–19 July 2021. This delay was expected, given the time required for news outlets to gather infor-
mation and send reporters to flood‐affected areas. The weekly number of FIS reduced by one order of magnitude
3 months later. While the FIS magnitude faded out in Bayern (BY) and Baden‐Württemberg (BW), it persisted for
the extreme events in North‐Rhine Westphalia (NW) and Rheinland‐Pfalz (RP) (Figure 6b ). The FIS magnitude
decreased for most impact classes over time. However, its share remained high months after the floods for some
impact classes (Figure S7 in Supporting Information S1 ).

Valley event. Yet, they caused local disruptions and should not be dismissed. For instance, 53 fire brigades and
more than 650 emergency services were called due to flooding in the east of BY from 05.07.2021 to 07.07.2021.

4. Limitations and Further Research Directions
Notwithstanding the advancements of our approach, it also has its limitations. First, the typology of impact
classes, while comprehensive, is not exhaustive. By following both an inductive (i.e., expert‐based) and deductive
approach (i.e., data‐driven), we identified 20 key impact classes (see Figure 2 ). Although we aimed for thor-
oughness, other researchers might suggest additional classes, such as infectious diseases or impacts to forestry.
However, this risk is low since the topic model results (Table S2 in Supporting Information S1 ) and the manual
annotation of 640 articles did not reveal any other significant impact classes with substantial document repre-
sentation. Therefore, even if new classes were proposed, they likely would not account for many impacts.
Second, our method assigns the detected impacts (i.e., FIS) to the multiple locations mentioned in an article. For
instance, if one article mentions that impact x occurred in cities A and B, and impact y occurred only in city B, our
approach assigns both impacts to both cities. However, we expect that this approach does not significantly affect
overall trends since most articles refer to only one location (mode = 1, average = 1.67, standard deviation = 0.97)
and one impact (mode = 0, average = 1.34, standard deviation 1.58). Moreover, since we used a clustering al-
gorithm and assigned impact locations only within the same spatial cluster, the results still represent regional
trends, as can be seen in our validation results (Figures 3–5 ). The use of large language models is a promising way
forward to address this issue (Li et al., 2024 ).
A third limitation refers to the size of the annotation data set, which influences the results of the machine‐learning
models. As shown in Table S5 in Supporting Information S1 , the number of annotated articles directly affects the
F1‐score, indicating that 50 positive labels per each impact class are a very good basis, but likely insufficient for
high performance. This is supported by the findings of previous research by Sodoge et al. ( 2023 ), who found that
at least 100 labels are needed for detecting drought impacts from newspaper text. In future studies, techniques
such as active learning (Jacobs et al., 2022 ) and few‐shot learning (Gupta et al., 2020 ) could be used to reduce the
need for extensive labeled data sets. Given the excellent performance of the dictionary‐based model (average
accuracy of 98% and F1‐score of 91%), we used it throughout the analysis. This high performance is due to the
extensive set of keywords used, derived based on previous studies (e.g., Alderman et al., 2012 ; de Brito
et al., 2020 ; Saulnier et al., 2017 ; Yari et al., 2020 ), expert knowledge, and various NLP methods (see detailed
description in Section 2.4 ).
Finally, newspaper coverage biases could have affected the obtained FIS data set. In fact, previous research has
shown that some topics may be overlooked or exaggerated depending on the newspaper's political orientation
(Bohr, 2020 ; Boykoff & Boykoff, 2004 ; Sodoge et al., 2024 ). To address this, we considered a broad array of
newspaper sources ( n = 250) covering different political orientations, spatial scales, and geographical regions
(Table S1 in Supporting Information S1 ). We also removed duplicate articles to minimize overreporting of
extreme impacts.
5. Conclusions
In this study, we assessed the magnitude of the socioeconomic impacts of the 2021 floods in Germany using a
large corpus of newspaper articles published from about 2 weeks before up to 6 months after the main flood event
in the Ahr Valley. Our empirical results revealed that the floods had far‐reaching impacts across multiple sectors,
underscoring the complex interplay between socioeconomic and infrastructure systems. The bulk of impacts
results largely from “damage to buildings” and “transport and mobility” FIS, which triggered cascading effects in
other sectors and infrastructure.
Our estimates show evidence for enhanced reporting of FIS in Ahrweiler, Euskirchen, and surrounding districts.
In these regions, the FIS magnitude remained high even months after the event, which may indicate the lasting
impacts of floods. Notably, we also detected small‐scale events with fewer FIS in the south and east of Germany.
These events were overlooked in previous studies and often go unnoticed in the national media or official disaster
reports (e.g., Junghänel et al., 2021 ) and databases such as HANZE (Paprotny et al., 2018 ) and EM‐Dat ( 2021 ).
