Differential Exposure to Climate Change? Evidence from the
2021 Floods in Germany
Moritz Odersky1 ·Max Löﬄer2
Received: 1 March 2023 / Accepted: 1 November 2023 / Published online: 11 January 2024
© The Author(s) 2024
Abstract
We analyze the exposure of different income groups to the 2021 ﬂoods in Germany,
which serve as an exemplary case of natural disasters intensiﬁed by anthropogenic climate
change. To this end, we link ofﬁcial geo-coded satellite data on ﬂood-affected buildings to
neighborhood-level information on socio-economic status. We then document the empiri-
cal relationship between ﬂood damages and household income. We limit comparisons to
the vicinity of affected rivers and absorb a rich set of regional ﬁxed effects to assess the
differential exposure at the local level. Average household income is around 1,500 euros
or three percent lower in ﬂood-affected neighborhoods than in non-affected neighborhoods
nearby. Average ﬂood exposure is more than three times as high in the bottom sixty than in
the upper forty percent of neighborhoods in terms of average household income. Our study is
the ﬁrst to document this regressive exposure along the income distribution based on actual
ﬂood damage data in Europe.
Keywords Climate change · Differential exposure · Floods · Income distribution
1 Introduction
In recent years the detrimental effects of climate change became increasingly evident. Rising
numbers of devastating wildﬁres, droughts, heat waves, storms, and ﬂoods were recorded in
all parts of the world. Natural disasters are expected to further intensify throughout the next
decades. Y et, little is known about the differential exposure to such climate change conse-
quences: Which socio-economic groups suffer to what degree from the environmental haz-
ards? Answering this question is essential for local policymakers to design welfare-optimizing
climate protection and adaption policies. The exposure of different socio-economic groups to
environmental damages is ultimately an empirical question since it depends on households’
location choices and the local incidence of climate-change related events.

In this paper, we investigate the distributional dimension of climate change by studying the
exposure to the 2021 ﬂoods in Germany along the income distribution. Multiple days of heavy
rainfall in July 2021 led to unprecedented water levels and ﬂoods across several rivers causing
more than 180 deaths, damaging thousands of buildings, and affecting around 40,000 people
in Germany alone (Fekete and Sandholz 2021). The monetary damage is estimated at around
33 billion euros (Munich Re 2022). These ﬂoods were the most damaging natural disaster
in Germany in decades. Although single weather events cannot be causally attributed to
climate change, we consider the 2021 ﬂoods to be a relevant case study since anthropogenic
climate change has increased the probability of such ﬂooding by 1.2-9 times (Tradowsky et al.
2023). Climate change worsens ﬂoods through two effects (Schäfer et al. 2021). First, global
warming leads to an increased abundance of water vapor in the atmosphere. Second, climate
change slows the polar jet stream causing weather systems to remain longer above certain
locations. Both effects severely increase the likelihood of heavy and long-lasting rainfall
events such as the 2021 ﬂoods (IPCC 2021). Accordingly, the German Federal Minister for
the Environment at the time declared: “Climate change has arrived in Germany” (Schulze
2021).
We descriptively analyze the exposure to the 2021 ﬂoods, combining detailed satellite and
socio-economic data for the affected German regions. We link ofﬁcial geo-coded satellite data
on ﬂood-affected buildings from the European Union’s Copernicus Emergency Management
Service to neighborhood-level information on household’s socio-economic status collected
by the geo-marketing provider microm. We then regress various measures of socio-economic
status on ﬂood damage indicators to understand the empirical relationship between both
variables. We call the resulting estimate the income-ﬂood-damage gradient.
A simple cross-sectional correlation between income and ﬂood damages across neigh-
borhoods is likely to pick up a variety of underlying explanations. For instance, if ﬂoods
had only occurred in the high-income metropolitan areas along the Rhine, a comparison
between affected neighborhoods and national average incomes or neighborhoods from far
distant regions would mainly be informed by macro-level differences in economic, cultural,
or political fundamentals. Instead, we are interested in the distributional incidence within the
same region. We argue that this local perspective is relevant for two reasons: First, it is more
informative for state and local policymakers concerned with inequality within the region.
Second, from an academic perspective, we know little about inequality and sorting within
small-scale regions in Germany so far. We limit our analysis to a within-region approach
in two ways: First, we restrict the comparison to neighborhoods in close proximity by only
comparing ﬂooded and non-ﬂooded neighborhoods within small buffer zones around affected
rivers. Second, we include a rich set of spatially ﬁne-grained ﬁxed effects for different regional
entities to absorb mean differences across regions. In our baseline speciﬁcation, we limit the
analysis to neighborhoods within three kilometers from affected rivers and within the same
county to make the comparison highly local. This modelling choice limits the inﬂuence of
differences in economic fundamentals or regional amenities and restricts the variation to
small-scale areas with comparable economic, cultural, and political environments.

buildings, average household income decreases by 21 euros. Vice versa, average ﬂood expo-

around seven to eight percent and thus more than three times as high as in neighborhoods

ﬁndings could be explained through various channels, such as the anecdotal evidence that
richer households tend to prefer the hillside and the periphery over historical centers in the val-
ley along the river because of better views and fewer building restrictions. Nonetheless, with
our descriptive analysis we cannot answer why the observed spatial sorting arose. We corrob-
orate the estimated empirical relationship by looking at various alternative socio-economic
measures such as private and supplementary insurance take-up, the average default risk, and
local unemployment rates, which all suggest that income and socio-economic status is lower
in more damaged neighborhoods. Importantly, these differences are not caused by the ﬂoods
but are observed in pre-ﬂood data from 2019 as well as 2010. Moreover, we show that they
cannot be explained simply by distance from the affected river or damaged buildings. We
also demonstrate the robustness of these results by testing for potential confounders with
even more ﬁne-grained regional indicators, and by using various alternative speciﬁcations.
Our paper speaks to the literature on the distributional effects of natural disasters. Several
studies investigated the link between environmental damages and socio-economic inequality,
mainly for the U.S. and Asia. A survey for the U.S. (Fothergill and Peek2004, p. 89) suggests
that the poor are “more vulnerable to natural disasters.” (Masozera et al. 2007, p. 299) ﬁnd
that Hurricane Katrina affected New Orleans neighborhoods “regardless of income.” Kahn
and Smith ( 2017) conclude similarly that high ﬂood-risk areas in the U.S. are not selective
with respect to income. In contrast, Bui et al. ( 2014, p. 1751) conclude that natural disasters
in Vietnam “worsen poverty and inequality.” Warr and Aung (2019) ﬁnd that environmental
damages reduced inequality between regions in Myanmar while raising inequality within
affected regions. Hsiang et al. ( 2019) also calls for a more nuanced view and concludes
that the poor are more exposed to some environmental damages but not necessarily climate
change.
Since differential exposure depends on local (dis)amenities as well as households’ pref-
erences, constraints, and location choices, which may all differ across regions and countries,
we argue it is also important to investigate this question in Europe. Floods also vary across
regions, for instance, between riverine and coastal areas. Poussard et al. ( 2021) study the
population living in ﬂoodplains in the province of Liege in Belgium and conclude that lower
middle class households have the highest ﬂood risk exposure compared to other income
groups. Osberghaus (2021) and Tovar Reaños ( 2021) evaluate imputed ﬂood risk indicators
over the income distribution in Germany and show that expected absolute ﬂood damage
increases with income whereas it declines strongly as a share of income; ﬂoods would thus
be regressive.
Our analysis contributes to this literature in three ways. First, we provide detailed empirical
evidence from a large region in Germany, which offers a setting that is more representative
for Europe than previous studies on the U.S. or Asia. Second, we investigate an actual
natural disaster using ofﬁcial data on damaged buildings rather than imputing ﬂood risks
and evaluating damage forecasts. Third, the hazardous event under evaluation was the most
severe ﬂooding in Western Europe since decades and therefore constitutes an exemplary case
to study the consequences of anthropogenic climate change.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces the data sets
used. In Section 3 we explain the empirical methodology we employ. We present the results
of our analysis in Section 4 and discuss the implications in Section 5. Section 6 concludes.

2 Data
The analysis combines two main data sources. We introduce the satellite data on ﬂood dam-
ages in Section 2.1. In Section 2.2, we provide details on the socio-economic data we employ
to compare socio-economic indicators at the neighborhood level. We then describe the sample
design for our baseline analysis in Section 2.3.
2.1 The 2021 floods and damaged buildings
The EU’s Copernicus Emergency Management Service provides publicly accessible geo-
spatial data for natural hazards and humanitarian catastrophes (CEMS 2023). The data sets
are based on satellite imagery, which is analysed and published by the CEMS ( 2022). For
the ﬂoods in the summer of 2021, the CEMS Rapid Mapping Service composed the data set
EMSR517 Flood in Western Germany consisting of multiple shapeﬁles each depicting one
broad regional area of ﬂood damage (area of interest). The data set is considered “closed” by
now, i.e., the content will not be altered anymore (CEMS2021). For the purpose of our study,
we combine the available shapeﬁles and plot all covered rivers, streams, and tributaries in the
two German states North Rhine-Westphalia and Rhineland-Palatinate that caused ﬂooding
during the summer of 2021. The resulting data set includes nine distinct areas of interest as
depicted in Panel A of Fig. 1.
Within these areas of interest the data set shows all buildings identiﬁed as affected by the
2021 ﬂoods (CEMS 2022). The CEMS determined the damage to a house through visual
inspection of satellite images. Whereas this method is fairly reliable in determining destruc-
tion, the CEMS also reports damaged and possibly damaged buildings. In the latter case, the
houses were reached by the water levels during the ﬂoods, but it is unclear to what degree
structural damage exists. In our baseline analysis, we pool the three categories and treat all
ﬂood-exposed buildings as damaged. We check the sensitivity of our results to this assump-
tion in Section 4.2 and show that results are largely similar when focusing on either of the
damage categories. Panel B of Fig. 1 plots an example of the satellite data for Bad Neuenahr-
Ahrweiler, one of the heavily affected areas. To test the spatial accuracy of the geo-coded
buildings, we compare the reported damage points to OpenStreetMap cartography of the
city, which reveals a high precision. Importantly, the CEMS shapeﬁles distinguish accurately
between exposed and non-exposed buildings instead of reporting all buildings in proximity
to a ﬂooded water body as being equally affected. This can be seen in the bottom of Panel B,
which shows that most buildings north of the river Ahr were damaged or destroyed but only
few buildings south of the river were affected.

3 Methodology
This section outlines our methodological approach to assess the differential exposure to the
2021 ﬂoods. We ﬁrst introduce the empirical speciﬁcation and explain the measurement of
the treatment variable, followed by a discussion of the underlying identifying assumptions.
Empirical speciﬁcation Our analysis rests on cross-sectional comparisons at the neighbor-
hood level, regressing average disposable household income in neighborhood i (denoted
by Inc o me
i ) on a treatment variable, Flood Damage i , while absorbing regional ﬁxed
effects:
Inc o mei = β · Flood Damage i + δa(i) + μc(i) + εi, (1)
with δa(i) and μc(i) referring to sets of indicator variables for areas of interest and counties,
respectively. The error term is denoted by εi . We measure the exposure to the 2021 ﬂoods
in two alternative ways: In a ﬁrst set of regressions, we dichotomize treatment by coding
ﬂood damage equal to one if any building in neighborhood i was affected, and equal to zero
otherwise. Second, in alternative speciﬁcations using a continuous treatment, we recode ﬂood
damage as the share of ﬂooded buildings in neighborhood i.

Regional confounders The interpretation of ˆβ as an estimate of the income-ﬂood-damage
gradient rests on the absence of omitted variables in the cross-sectional regression, which
is arguably a strong assumption. To illustrate this, consider the raw correlation between
income and ﬂood damage without accounting for regional ﬁxed effects or restricting the
sample to speciﬁc areas. Such a regression comparing neighborhoods from far distant regions
would pick up broad economic and cultural differences across space rather than informing
us about the spatial sorting of households within small local areas. Prime examples for such
unobserved factors would be differences in economic, cultural, or political fundamentals.
Imagine we compared neighborhoods all across Germany but the ﬂoods occurred only in
the high-income metropolitan areas along the Rhine. The resulting estimate would largely
be informed by regional differences in productivity or amenities, which should capitalize in
earnings (see the literature on local labor markets and Moretti 2011 for an overview). Since
neighborhoods close to the Rhine would also be more likely to be affected by the ﬂoods, this
would feed and largely drive our estimate ˆβ.

Research design To mitigate the impact of such differences in fundamentals, we restrict
our analysis to a within-region comparison of ﬂood-damaged neighborhoods with close-by,
non-ﬂooded areas. Our approach involves three steps to improve on the raw correlation across
space. First, we only include grid cells within three-kilometer buffers around the affected
river sections in our sample. Figure 2 shows an example. The distance of 3 km ensures
close proximity between damaged and non-damaged neighborhoods while still providing a
sufﬁciently large sample size (we also test the robustness of our results by using narrower
bands).
Second, we add ﬁxed effects for areas of interest (see the term δ
a(i) in Eq. 1). Comparing
only proximate ﬂooded and non-ﬂooded neighborhoods within the same area of interest
limits the potential for unobserved confounders. Economic fundamentals like productive or
consumption amenities as well as cultural determinants should be largely similar within these
areas along the same river. Moreover, areas of interest are economically highly integrated
because of low commuting, transport, and transaction costs. Neighborhoods can thus be
expected to be quite comparable except for the fact that some are more prone to ﬂooding.
Third, we further tighten the identiﬁcation by absorbing mean differences across economic
regions and administrative jurisdictions. Most of the broader regional differences are already
omitted from the analysis via the use of ﬁxed effects for areas of interest. However, some of
the affected areas of interest span across multiple commuting zones or even states. Comparing
neighborhoods within these areas would thus also pick up differences across different labor
markets or legislative entities. In our baseline speciﬁcation, we include an additional set of
county ﬁxed effects (μ
c(i) in Eq. 1; Kreise und kreisfreie Städte) to further limit the inﬂuence
of different economic circumstances or policy regimes across space.
To cross-check our results, we also employ various alternative indicators for socio-
economic status instead of looking at household income. We therefore use measures of
private and supplementary insurance take-up, the average household default-risk rating, the
local unemployment rate, and a set of demographic variables as outcomes in Eq. 1.

As discussed before, the estimated gap in household income may partly be due to other fac-
tors such as the degree of urbanization, local economic fundamentals, or cultural differences.
Depending on the relative importance of these channels, the raw cross-sectional relationship
may over- or underestimate the true income-ﬂood-damage gradient at the local level. We
consecutively add regional ﬁxed effects to limit the variation to neighborhoods from the
same region instead of comparing far distant grid cells. In Column (2), we include indicators
for the nine areas of interest, which widens the estimated gap to 1,678 euros lower average
income in neighborhoods affected by the ﬂoods. As areas of interest partly span across labor
markets as well as states, we additionally absorb mean differences between six metropolitan
statistical areas ( Raumordnungsregionen) and 13 commuting zones ( Arbeitsmarktregionen)
in Columns (3) and (4), respectively.
In our preferred speciﬁcation in Column (5), we add county ﬁxed effects to account
for economic, cultural, and political differences between the 18 affected counties ( Kreise
und kreisfreie Städte ). Results remain similar. The estimates show that average disposable

4.2 Margins of exposure and further analyses
In the preceding analyses, we treated each building as being either affected or not, irre-
spective of its degree of damage. We now ﬁrst turn to different margins of exposure at the
building level. To this end, we make use of the damage categories reported in the CEMS
data. Each affected building is coded as either (i) destroyed, (ii) damaged, or (iii) possibly
damaged. In our baseline, we pool all three categories when measuring ﬂood exposure. This
procedure however does not differentiate between the extensive and intensive margin, and
123

[[PAGE 14]]
564 M. Odersky and M. Löfﬂer
thus ignores the degree of damage conditional on being affected. To distinguish between
both margins, we narrow the deﬁnition of ﬂood affected buildings by, ﬁrst, excluding pos-
sibly damaged buildings, and, second, restricting the treatment to destroyed houses only.
Columns (2) and (3) in Panel A of Table 3 provide the corresponding results. Results are
largely similar to our preferred speciﬁcation (replicated in Column (1)). If anything, focusing
on destroyed buildings only leads to more negative estimates, which suggests that there might
be an additional regressive intensive margin of ﬂood exposure. However, estimates are not
statistically different from each other.
We next turn to the neighborhood level and shed more light on the different margins of
exposure by excluding neighborhoods from the sample that had some but not many house-
holds affected by the ﬂoods. More precisely, we sequentially drop affected neighborhoods
with less than 60 percent, 80 percent, or 100 percent of buildings damaged, respectively, such
that we compare only highly-exposed and non-affected places. Columns (4)–(6) in Panel A of
Table 3 show the results. Estimates become more negative when focusing on largely affected
neighborhoods. However, estimates are again not statistically different across speciﬁcations.
These speciﬁcations are also informative regarding the uncertainty introduced by using aver-
age local instead of household-level income. If only a few buildings in a densely populated
neighborhood are exposed to the ﬂood, grid-level average income may be a coarse proxy
for the incomes of affected households. The larger the percentage of affected buildings in a
neighborhood, the more accurate it is. When we only look at cases where all buildings in a
neighborhood are affected, average income should be a good proxy for the income levels of
exposed households. Estimates become stronger, which further corroborates our results (see
Column (6)).
The role of distance One explanation for the estimated income-ﬂood-damage gradient could
be that it simply reﬂects income differences related to the distance to a river. Since all exposed
buildings reported by the CEMS are located within one kilometer from affected water bodies,
our baseline estimate may just pick up the effect of distance to rivers. To test this alternative
hypothesis, we ﬁrst assess whether the three kilometer buffers are too broad and limit our
baseline sample to neighborhoods within 2 km (1 km) buffers. Second, we introduce indicator
variables for distance bands in the regression, which absorb buffer ﬁxed effects ( ≤ 1k m ,
(1,2] km, (2,3] km). Third, we linearly control for distance. Columns (7)–(10) in Panel B of
Table 3 depict the results. Estimates are largely similar but become somewhat smaller in size
when we tighten buffers around affected rivers. We thus conclude that distance to the river can
only explain a small portion of the estimated income-ﬂood-damage gradient. Extending the
buffer area beyond three kilometers distance would likely lead to bigger differences and thus
more negative results given that our baseline sample depicts lower average income compared
to surrounding municipalities (see Appendix Fig. A.1).
Another underlying driver could be that we compare different segments along the same
river, e.g., upstream vs. downstream neighborhoods, both of which would still be within the
deﬁned buffer zones. However, they could be somewhat distant from each other and thus
also different in fundamentals, which may only be partly picked up by the sets of regional
ﬁxed effects. To further investigate this concern, we calculate for each neighborhood the
distance to the nearest affected building reported in the CEMS data set. The median and
mean distance amount to three and four kilometer, respectively. No neighborhood in our
sample is further away than 21 km from an affected building. Figure4 illustrates the resulting
measure using binned scatter plots and focusing on neighborhoods within six kilometer to

5 Discussion
The empirical results document that neighborhoods with lower average income were dis-
proportionally exposed to the ﬂoods. Affected neighborhoods display around 1,500 euros
lower average disposable household incomes, which corresponds to a three percent differ-
ence compared to the mean. For each percentage point difference in the share of ﬂood-affected
buildings, we ﬁnd that neighborhood-level average incomes are around 21 euros lower. Ana-
lyzing the exposure of neighborhoods along the distribution of average incomes, we also ﬁnd
a regressive pattern: The poorest sixty percent of neighborhoods display two times higher
shares of ﬂood damaged buildings than neighborhoods in the upper forty percent.
Our results further provide suggestive evidence for an intensive margin of ﬂood expo-
sure: Neighborhoods, where almost all houses were exposed, display even lower average
incomes than less-affected places. We conclude that exposure to the ﬂoods was regressive.
In terms of magnitude, the differences we observed are not huge but still sizable compared
to, e.g., the “rapid emergency aid” of 3,500 euros per household that were granted by the
state Rhineland-Palatinate in the aftermath of the ﬂoods ( Soforthilfe Privatpersonen ). The
differential exposure is further corroborated by indicative evidence that neighborhoods with
more exposed households display lower rates of private insurance take-up and have higher
average default risks. We also ﬁnd that affected neighborhoods are inhabited by fewer chil-
dren, more elderly, and more households with foreign background compared to unaffected
places nearby. In the following section, we compare discuss our results , their implications,
and how they compare to the existing literature and discuss their implications.
Comparison to previous literature The overrepresentation of lower income neighbor-
hoods in the ﬂood areas is in line with the previous literature based on ﬂood risk forecasts
(Osberghaus 2021; Tovar Reaños 2021). To the best of our knowledge, our study is the
ﬁrst analysis based on actual damage data that indicates a higher ﬂood exposure for poorer
households in Europe. The observed disproportionate exposure of the elderly is in line with
international evidence as well as during the Elbe ﬂood in 2002 in Saxony (Lieberman-
Cribbin et al. 2021; Kuhlicke et al. 2011). The results on the proportion of households with
foreign background are also in line with the existing international literature on racial and
ethnic minorities (Fielding 2018;T a t ee ta l .2019). Our ﬁnding of a lower share of children in
more affected neighborhoods partly contradicts previous research: (Tovar Reaños2021) ﬁnds
higher exposure of families based on ﬂood risk indicators. Reasons for the conﬂicting results
could be differences in the variable deﬁnition (“population below age 18” vs. “families”) or
the regional composition of the samples.
Our results are purely descriptive and we thus cannot explain the underlying drivers.
Nonetheless, the observed empirical relationships are in line with anecdotal evidence that
richer households rather avoid the historical centers in the valley along the river and move

to the hillside or the periphery (see, e.g., local newsreports such as in Die Rheinpfalz 2015).
Another possible channel is the capitalization of ﬂood risk into local property prices. While
ﬂood risk is often undervalued in property prices, several studies have found a signiﬁcant
discount in various countries including Germany (see, e.g., Kousky,2010; Atreya et al.,2013;
Zhang and Leonard 2019). Lower prices might attract households with lower socio-economic
status, which could drive our results. Although we cannot provide causal evidence on the
channels behind the observed differences, the descriptive results themselves have important
policy implications. To this end, we next relate our results to the concept of vulnerability and
discuss potential policy implications with a focus on public and private ﬂood insurance.
Dimensions of vulnerability Our ﬁnding of a higher exposure of low-income groups speaks
to the debate on the dimensions of population’s vulnerability to environmental hazards (ch. 7
UNEP 2007). The three dimensions are related to (i) exposure, (ii) sensitivity, and (iii) ability
to cope and recover. The estimated negative income-ﬂood-damage gradient shows that the ﬁrst
of these dimensions is particularly strong for households from low-income neighborhoods.
To understand the total impact of the 2021 ﬂoods on the population, it would be necessary to
assess how sensitive and able to recover the disproportionally exposed groups are. Our data
does not allow to shed further light on the latter two dimensions of vulnerability. Whereas
sensitivity may be unrelated to income, there is most likely a positive correlation between
income and the ability to cope and recover as the availability of ﬁnancial resources is a key
element of the coping capacity (Truedinger et al. 2023). One instrument to raise the coping
capacity and thus reduce vulnerability of low-income households are private or public ﬂood
insurance institutions.
Flood insurance: the status quoCurrently, ﬂood insurance in Germany is offered via private
insurance companies (the particular insurance type is usually labeled asElementar-/erweiterte
Naturgefahrenversicherung). Buying insurance is voluntarily and only 44 percent of the resi-
dential houses were covered against ﬂood damage in 2012–14 (Osberghaus2021). Premiums
are risk-based and barely any subsidization between risk groups takes place. Consequently,
insurance premia in ﬂood risk areas are high, which may explain why low-income groups are
subsequently less often insured against ﬂoods than wealthier households (Osberghaus and
Hünewaldt 2023). With increasing ﬂood risk due to climate change, premiums are expected
to rise further, likely leading to a decline in voluntary insurance coverage among low-income
groups (Qiang 2019; Tesselaar et al 2020). In addition, people were repeatedly found to
underestimate ﬂood risk, resulting in underinsurance. Even in the highly exposed Ahr V al-
ley, there is little indication for high salience of ﬂood risks after the ﬂoods. Recent survey
evidence shows that half of the respondents who were affected by the 2021 ﬂoods “consider
such an extreme ﬂood very unlikely” in the future (Truedinger et al. 2023, p. 13).
Given the low ﬂood insurance take-up, there have often been strong calls for discre-
tionary public recovery funds after ﬂood events. In response to the 2021 ﬂoods, the federal
German government created a 30 billion euros reconstruction fund to rebuild infrastructure
and provide ﬁnancial support for un(der)insured ﬁrms and households (German Bundestag
2021). Households can receive ﬁnancial compensation for up to 80 percent of the damage to
their property (BMF 2021). As all households can request compensation for 80 percent of
the damage regardless of the total amount, richer households with more valuable dwellings
receive higher absolute compensations. Moreover, research on post-disaster response policies
showed that lower income groups often experience greater difﬁculties to apply and receive
ﬁnancial aid (Grube et al. 2018; Muñoz and Tate 2016; Fothergill and Peek 2004). Among
other examples, research has shown that after Hurricane Katrina signiﬁcantly fewer public

resources went to disadvantaged groups (Islam and Winkel 2017; Masozera et al. 2007). We
thus expect that households from lower-income neighborhoods receive less compensation
for damages from private insurances and the state.
Policy implicationsThe current situation creates a moral hazard problem: Discretionary post-
disaster recovery payments do not incentivize climate change adaptation but rather encourage
reconstruction of buildings in the same ﬂood risk areas. Rising climate change is likely to
further aggravate this problem. Instead of relying on ad-hoc public recovery funds, legislators
could introduce an insurance mandate for homeowners against natural disasters. An insurance
mandate combined with the actuarially fair ﬂood-risk dependent premia would improve
economic efﬁciency: Flood risk would capitalize in property prices and maintenance costs,
discouraging development in ﬂood risk areas and incentivizing climate change adaptation.
Such a policy response was also frequently discussed in the immediate aftermath of severe
ﬂoods in Germany.
However, it has not yet been implemented. A core argument against such a policy is that
households may not be able to afford actuarially fair premia. Indeed, our empirical ﬁnding of
differential exposure along the income distribution illustrates that households in low-income
neighborhoods would have to pay some of the highest premia in a risk-dependent pricing
system, which may raise fairness concerns. Our results thus highlight an equity-efﬁciency
trade-off for welfare-optimizing policy responses.
Instruments to mute the distributive effects of an insurance mandate are manifold. For
instance, legislators could lower insurance costs for low-income, high-risk households by
introducing income-dependent tax credits or subsidies. Policymakers may also regulate insur-
ance companies and oblige them to charge equal prices and thus redistribute between high
and low ﬂood risk households. However, such measures would come at the cost of weakening
price signals of ﬂood risk and thereby discourage climate change adaptation. Another sup-
port measure could therefore be to offer ﬁnancial means to low-income households in order
to move to lower-risk places. Y et another instrument to address the policy trade-off would
be tax-ﬁnanced investments in ﬂood protection and management infrastructure. The optimal
policy ultimately depends, of course, on fairness preferences and social welfare weights.
6 Conclusion
In this paper, we investigate the differential exposure to climate change and ask which socio-
economic groups suffer most from its consequences. We study the case of the 2021 ﬂoods in
Germany, one of the most damaging natural disasters in Western Europe in recent decades.
Our analysis combines geo-coded post-disaster satellite data on actual ﬂood damages with
highly disaggregated data on average incomes for 1 km
2 areas. To identify the income-ﬂood-
damage gradient and account for potential confounders due to broad economic or cultural
differences across regions, we focus on neighborhoods in close proximity to affected rivers
and include various regional ﬁxed effects. Our empirical analysis documents that neighbor-
hoods with lower average disposable household income were disproportionally exposed to
the 2021 ﬂoods. Therefore, exposure to the ﬂoods was regressive.
A methodological limitation of our study is that we rely on aggregated neighborhood-
level income data. Ideally, we would evaluate the income-ﬂood-damage gradient based on
administrative household-level data. Besides bringing more precision, such a data base would
allow to study heterogeneous effects along socio-economic and demographic characteristics

574 M. Odersky and M. Löfﬂer
in more detail. Along these lines, it would also be interesting to investigate differences in
wealth rather than income, which we cannot do because of data unavailability. Another
limitation is the case-study nature of our analysis. Although the ﬂoods were one of the most
disastrous natural hazards in Europe in recent decades, they are still a unique event. Settlement
patterns may be different for other regions and thus have to be studied case-by-case.
