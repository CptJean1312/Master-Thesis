The inﬂuence of land use patterns on stream water quality is scale-dependent in space and time. Understanding
the relationship between landscape characteristics and water quality is of great importance to improve water
contamination prediction in unmonitored watersheds and for providing guidelines for watershed land use plan-
ning. In this study, ﬁve water sampling sites in the upper Dan River basin were established to monitor seasonal
water chemical contamination over the period of 2000 to 2008. The relationships between land use patterns and
water quality were analyzed across multiple-scales using redundancy analysis. The results showed that stream
water quality variables displayed highly temporal variations, with electrical conductivity (EC), ammonium nitro-
gen (NH
4
+-N), nitrate nitrogen (NO3
−
N), and total suspended solids (TSS) all generally displaying higher levels in
the wet season, while there were higher concentrations of biochemical oxygen demand (BOD5), chemical oxygen
demand (CODcr), and dissolved oxygen (DO) in the dry season. The total contribution of land use patterns on
overall water quality was stronger at the riparian scale than at the catchment and reach scales during the wet sea-
son. However, different land use metrics had different scale effects. Urban land had a higher positive relationship
with degraded water quality at small scales than at large scales, whereas agricultural land displayed the opposite
scale effects. Forest and grassland explained more water quality variations at the riparian scale than at other
scales. Analyses of spatial development patterns suggested that size, density, aggregation, and diversity of land-
scape patterns were important factors impacting on stream water quality. The results provide important infor-
mation regarding sustainable land use and landscape planning at multiple-scales that can be used to improve
water quality.

1. Introduction
Water quality plays a pivotal role in habitat protection, agriculture,
industry, and public health (Akasaka et al., 2010; Lu et al., 2015). Main-
taining water quality is challenging, primarily due to point source (PS)
and non-point source (NPS) pollution. PS pollution mainly includes in-
dustrial and domestic wastewater loads, which can be relatively easily
identiﬁed (Wang et al., 2016). Unlike PS pollution, NPS pollution usually
originates from diffuse sources, such as agriculture, street runoff, and
the deposition of atmospheric pollutants (Ongley et al., 2010). The com-
plex interaction between rainfall and landscape characteristics in-
creases the difﬁculty of NPS identiﬁcation (Liu et al., 2016).
The inﬂuence of land use on water quality has been a concern since
the 1970s (Rimer et al., 1978). Early studies correlated water chemistry
with different land use types within a watershed ( Bolstad and Swank,
1997; Donohue et al., 2006). The improper land use may amplify trans-
ference of nutrients to water bodies ( Valle Junior et al., 2014; Pacheco
and Fernandes, 2016). Intense agricultural activities and rapid urbaniza-
tion created an immense pressure on water quality. The percentage
cover of agricultural land is signi ﬁcantly positively correlated with
water pollution due to fertilizer application entering surface water
through runoff (Tu, 2011). Urbanization is associated with changes in
land uses through infrastructure development. As cities expand, the in-
crease in impervious surfaces (i.e., roads, roof tops, and parking lots) re-
sults to an increase in runoff, which in turn creates additional avenues
for the transportation of NPS pollutants to rivers ( Wilson and Weng,
2010). While, forest lands were considered as net sinks of nitrate
(Pacheco et al., 2015 ). Land use composition is generally linked with
water quality within a watershed, and landscape con ﬁgurations may
be more sensitive predictors of water quality. Recently, with the rapid
development of Geographic Information System (GIS) technology and
landscape ecology, landscape metrics have provided a useful approach
to quantify land use con ﬁgurations. Sun et al. (2013) and Bu et al.
(2014) found that landscape metrics such as aggregation (refers to the

Landscape metrics are sensitive to change with spatial pattern, and
the scale dependence is referred to as grain size and spatial extent
(Wu et al., 2002). Three spatial scales including reach, catchment and ri-
parian have been widely used to relate landscape variables to stream
water quality: (1) reach is usually described by a buffer of several hun-
dred meters in width on the studying site; (2) riparian is often deﬁned
as a buffer with a similar or larger width compared to reach extending
to the entire upstream above the studying site; (3) catchment is typical-
ly the entire catchment upstream above the studying site (Allan, 2004;
Ding et al., 2016 ). However, the results have not consistently deter-
mined which spatial extent is the best predictor of water quality.
Some studies have indicated that the entire catchment was important
for determining the effects of anthropogenic activities or water quality
(King et al., 2005; Meneses et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2016 ), while other
studies have found that land use at the reach or riparian scale better
accounted for water quality variability ( Sahu and Gu, 2009; Collins
et al., 2013; McMillan et al., 2014). Riparian buffer has a ﬁltering ability
to remove sediment from land runoff (Santos et al., 2015). These incon-
sistent results were attributed to the unique characteristics of each
catchment that in ﬂuenced water quality ( Sliva and Williams, 2001 ).
Thus, more research is needed to quantify the effect of different land
use scales on water quality to improve water resource management.
Water scarcity is one of the most important issues in China. To re-
lieve water shortages in the north and northwest of China, three routes
(west, middle, and east) of the South to North Water Transfer Project
(SNWTP) were established in 2002 ( Zhang, 2005 ). The Dan River is
the water source area of the middle route of the SNWTP, supplying
water to Beijng and Tianjing in northern China for drinking, industrial
uses, and agricultural purposes ( Li et al., 2009 ). Thus, maintaining
good water quality in the basin is of great importance for domestic
economic development and social stability. Previous studies have re-
ported that NPS pollution in the Dan River basin has increased dramat-
ically due to intensive anthropogenic activities since the 1980s (Li et al.,
2008; Ai et al., 2015), although there is still a lack of information regard-
ing the spatial and seasonal patterns in water contamination in this
basin. The objectives of this paper were to: (1) present the spatial and
temporal variability of stream water contamination in the Dan River
basin, (2) quantify the relationship between watershed land use pat-
terns and water quality, and (3) identify the scale effects in measuring
the inﬂuence of land use patterns on water quality.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Study area
The Dan River basin is located in the southeast of Shaanxi Province,
China (33°12 ′–34°11′N, 109°30 ′-111°1′E) and covers an area of
1.68 × 10
4 km2, with a total length of 443 km ( Fig. 1). The watershed
has a typical subtropical monsoonal climate, with an annual cycle of
dry and wet seasons ( Ai et al., 2015 ). The average annual air tempera-
ture is 14 °C, and the average annual amount of sunshine is 1974 h
(Xu et al., 2013 ). The annual mean precipitation is 800 mm, 60% of
which occurs in the summer months from July to September ( Fig. 2).
The streamﬂow mostly occurs during the wet season, while the dry sea-
son experiences lower ﬂows (Fig. 2). The topography in the watershed
is characterized by mountain ranges and hills, and the elevation ranges
from 203 to 2068 m ( Fig. 1). Soil types in this area are dominated by
yellow-brown soil (Haplumbrepts) and sandy soil (Cheng et al., 2016).
The natural vegetation in the area is dominated by coniferous,
broad-leaved, deciduous forest, and shrubs and herbs. The main
agricultural crops are corn ( Zea mays L.), rice (Oryza sativa L.), peanut
(Arachis hypogaea L.), and wheat ( Triticum aestivum L.).

2.2. Water sampling and analysis
Shaanxi Environmental Monitoring Centre has establishedﬁve sam-
pling sites in the Dan River to monitor water contamination ( Fig. 1 ).
Sites 1 and 2 are located in the upstream with little anthropogenic activ-
ity, while sites 3 and 4 are near Shangzhou City. Site 5 is located down-
stream of Shangzhou city. In the ﬁeld sampling, three equal-interval
verticals were set in the stream cross section and water was sampled
in the depth of 0.5 m below the surface water in each verticals. Water
samples were collected in the months of January, March, May, July, Sep-
tember and November from the year of 2000 to 2008. To analyze the
temporal variation, water chemistry data series were split into wet sea-
son, i.e., May, July and September, and dry season, i.e., January, March
and November. Eight water quality parameters, including pH, dissolved
oxygen (DO), electrical conductivity (EC), total suspended solids (TSS),
chemical oxygen demand (COD cr), nitrate nitrogen (NO 3(NH4
−-N),
ammoniam nitrogen (NH 4
+-N), and biochemical oxygen demand
(BOD5), were analyzed according to the environmental quality stan-
dards for surface water ( Chinese State Environmental Protection
Bureau, 2002 ). The analytical methods used were as follows: pH, EC,
and DO were determined on site using HQ30d; BOD 5, and COD cr were
analyzed using a dilution and seeding method and the potassium per-
manganate method, respectively (Rice et al., 2012 ); NO3(NH4
−-N and
NH4
+-N were measured using a colorimetric method ( Rice et al.,
2012); and TSS was calculated by a ﬁltering method (APHA, 2005).

2.4. Statistical analysis
The normality of the data was examined by Kolmogorov – Smirnov
tests and a log (x + 1) transformation of the BOD 5 was performed to
meet the assumptions of normality. The independent samples t-test
was used to detect temporal differences in water contamination con-
centrations. The one-way ANOVA was used to analyze the variance in
water quality in different sites. Redundancy analysis (RDA) is now
widely used to determine the relationship between environmental fac-
tors and landscape metrics ( Shen et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2016 ). RDA
was used to examine the water quality and landscape relationship at
multiple spatial scales using CANOCO 4.5 software. The arrows of two
variables pointing in the same direction indicate a positive correlation,
and the angle between two arrows is inversely proportional to the de-
gree of their correlation. The length of the arrow can be interpreted as
a mutual similarity of contributions ( Ding et al., 2016; Zhao et al.,
2015). The proportion of the water quality variance explained by the
land use metrics can also be obtained from RDA. A comprehensiveﬂow-
chart was designed to show variables used in RDA and how they were
combined with spatial and temporal analysis (Fig. 3).

3.3. The effect of land use and landscape pattern on water quality
To quantify the effect of land use and landscape pattern on water
quality, an RDA was conducted. The RDA results showed that the effect
of land use patterns on the overall water quality exhibited signi ﬁcant
seasonal differences. The predictors in the wet season explained N40%
of the variation in water quality, whereas this decreased by 10% in the
dry season (Table 5). The contributions of land use and landscape pat-
tern on water quality varied from the reach and riparian to catchment
scales. At the reach scale, the predictors explained 44.3% during the
wet season, but when the scale was increased to riparian this increased
to 68.5%. However, as the scale increased to catchment, the explanatory
ability decreased to 49.0%.
There were different scale effects for the different land use types.
During the wet season, 7.5% of the total variation in water quality
could be explained by the urban area at the reach scale, whereas this
land use explained only 4.1 and 1.1% when the scales were enlarged
to riparian and catchment, respectively ( Table 5). The same tendency
could be found in the dry season. This suggests that human settlement
near the river bank will have a strong impact on water quality. Particu-
larly, urban land cover was positively correlated with COD, TSS, and
NH4
+-N at the reach scale during the wet season (Fig. 6f). For agricultural
land, the effects of scale were the opposite in the wet season, with var-
iations in water quality being more dependent on this land use type at
the catchment scale than at the riparian and reach scales, and agricul-
tural land cover was positively correlated with pH, DO, and NO 3
−-N
(Fig. 6b). Forest and grasslands were the dominant factors controlling
water quality at the riparian scale, both of which had negative correla-

4. Discussion
4.1. Relationships between land use and water quality
This study showed that urban and agricultural land had negative ef-
fects on water quality, whereas forest and grassland had positive effects
on water quality. These results are consistent with most previous stud-
ies (Li et al., 2009; Tu, 2011; Wan et al., 2014 ). Compared with other
land uses, urban and agricultural lands were more likely to generate
NPS pollution. Urban land cover was positively correlated with a num-
ber of water quality parameters and the highest median concentrations
of TSS, CODcr,N O3
−-N, and NH4
+-N occurred at catchment-4, where the
majority of the urban area was located. Similarly,Tong and Chen (2002)
found that the stream water nitrogen concentration was signi ﬁcantly
positively related to commercial and residential land cover. NPS pollu-
tion (including nutrients, heavy metals, and organic compounds from
multifarious sources) in urban areas are transferred to streams via run-
off from impervious surfaces, which adversely affect stream water qual-
ity during and following precipitation events ( Johnson et al., 2013 ).
Agricultural land use strongly inﬂuences nutrient and sediment concen-
trations of stream water ( Li et al., 2008 ). It has been reported that the
agriculture contributes N 50% of the total load ( Ongley et al., 2010 ),
and the fraction of the total load is highly dependent on the proportion
of agriculture in the watershed. In agricultural regions, increasing inputs
of inorganic fertilizer, organic manure, and pesticides are the primary
factors that contribute to increased regional crop production ( Zhang
et al., 2014). Conventional agricultural activity (i.e., tillage or excessive
fertilization) increases the risk of soil erosion and excessive nutrient ap-
plications can result in NPS pollution to stream water following surface
runoff.

pollution ( Ouyang et al., 2010 ). Vegetation has been recognized as

4.2. Effects of landscape patterns on water quality
Landscape ecology focuses on spatial patterns, and the spatial con-
ﬁguration of landscape plays a critical role in determining ecological
processes such as nutrient cycles, hydrological process, and energy
ﬂows (Wu, 2013). The size, density, aggregation, and diversity of land-
scape patterns are signi ﬁcant land use characteristics that impact
stream water quality (Lee et al., 2009). In this study, most water quality
parameters were positively associated with PD and SHDI, whereas they
were negatively related to COHE, CONTAG, CONTIG, LPI, LSI, and SHAPE.
LSI and SHAPE are measures of the complexity of landscape patch
shapes. Human activities produce regular shapes, with straight borders,
by introducing rectangularity and rectilinearity to landscapes ( Moser
et al., 2002 ). In particular, cropland and residential areas usually have
straight and distinct boundaries, whereas natural landscapes such as
grassland and forest are normally characterized by irregular shapes
and boundaries. In this study, both LSI and SHAPE were negatively relat-
ed to COD, NH4
+-N, and TSS, suggesting that a more complex landscape
patch shape helps to retain more nutrients.
Degraded water quality was positively related to PD and SHDI. Lee

BOD, COD, and TN were positively related to PD. As a fragmentation
metric, a high degree of PD re ﬂects small patches of land use within a
watershed, which may increase soil erosion and surface runoff. SHDI re-
ﬂects the patch diversity within the watershed, which is greatly

4.3. The inﬂuence of spatial and temporal scales
The inﬂuence of land use and land cover patterns on water quality is
scale dependent (Pratt and Chang, 2012; Ding et al., 2016 ). The results
showed that the riparian scale was better than the catchment and
reach scales for explaining overall water quality variations. The riparian
zone acts as a ﬁlter by reducing surface runoff, retaining sediments, and
processing nutrients to improve stream water quality ( Miserendino
et al., 2011; Fernandes et al., 2014 ). Carey et al. (2011) suggested that
a riparian buffer was the key element in land use regulation. Buck
et al. (2004) reported that nutrient concentrations in stream water
were better predicted by riparian land use rather than catchment land
use, and suggested that the “small-scale” effects of local land manage-
ment should be given more attention. However, some studies have
shown that the watershed had a greater impact on water quality than
the riparian zone (Nash et al., 2009; Ding et al., 2016). This opposite re-
sult might be related to different watershed land use patterns or to
water quality parameters of interest.
It is worth noting that different land uses had various scale effects.
For example, urban land has greater impacts on water quality at smaller
scales. This result was also found in a previous study, where urban land
cover was positively correlated with water quality parameters at the ri-
parian scale (Pratt and Chang, 2012 ). This is likely because residential
areas are dispersed along the river (Fig. 4) and the artiﬁcial disturbances
caused by building greatly damage river ecosystems ( Wasson et al.,
2010). Agricultural land use is more strongly correlated with water
quality at larger scales, which may be the related to the wide distribu-
tion of cropland across the watershed (Fig. 4). The control of agricultural
NPS should focus on larger scales and catchment agricultural manage-
ment practices such as fertilizer use and conservation tillage. Forest
and grassland cover is highly correlated with improved water quality
at the riparian scale. Riparian zones function as an important connection
between terrestrial and aquatic systems ( Fernandes et al., 2014 ), and
protection of these area is critical for in-stream water quality. Riparian
vegetation restoration is important for the maintenance and improve-
ment of stream water quality, that vegetation in this area could decrease
pollution concentrations in stream water (de Souza et al., 2013).
Seasonal variations of precipitation and runoff have great effects on
stream water contamination concentrations. The concentration of
nutrients such as NH 4
+-N, NO 3
−-N, and TSS were higher in the wet
season than the dry season, which was consistent with previous studies
(Ai et al., 2015 ). In this study, the effects of land use on water quality
were more evident during the wet season. Bolstad and Swank (1997)
found that stream water quality had stronger relationships with land
use during storm ﬂow than during base ﬂow. Thus, the in ﬂuence of
land use on stream water quality is scale-dependent, and varies over
space and time.
4.4. Management suggestion
Urban areas in this basin were mainly dispersed along the river,
which had a negative effect on stream water quality. So, it is important
to control urban runoff, and make sure if meets national standards. Ag-
riculture land had a strong impact on water quality at a large scale dur-
ing the wet season. Ecological construction (i.e., terraced ﬁelds) and
conservation tillage (i.e., no-tillage) as well as precision fertilization
would help to reduce nutrients loss from cropland. Forest and grassland
had strong contributions to water quality variation at the riparian scale.
In this region, it is of signi ﬁcance to implement Green for Grain Project
to reduce soil erosion. Landscape pattern planning, such as increasing
patch boundary complexity, enhancing patch connectivity and decreas-
ing landscape fragmentation, should be considered to improve water-
shed water quality.
5. Conclusions
Stream water quality in the Dan River basin displayed signi ﬁcant
spatial and seasonal variations. Land use had a strong relationship
with water quality during the wet season. Following RDA, the explana-
tion of land use and land cover patterns on water quality was higher at
riparian scales than at the catchment and reach scales. However, differ-
ent land use metrics had different scale effects, suggesting that land use
planning in water quality management needs to adopt a multi-scale
perspective.
