Abstract
We study the effectiveness of the Mississippi River
Basin Healthy Watersheds Initiative (MRBI) in reduc-
ing nitrogen pollution in surface water bodies. We use
a Kitagawa-Oaxaca-Blinder (KOB) counterfactual
decomposition method to quantify the role of income
and policy in reducing nitrogen pollution in
waterbodies. Our results show that the MRBI policy for
the 2012 cohort of implemented watersheds across five
states (Arkansas, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, and Tennes-
see) experienced a 43% reduction in nitrogen concentra-
tions when comparing the 2009 –2011 pre-treatment
period to the 2012 –2018 post-treatment period. Decom-
position results show that 79% of the improvement in
water quality from policy treatment is derived from an
endowment effect, driven mainly by location-fixed
effects that include cross-sectional mean differences in
income, among other characteristics. Results also show
that 21% of differences are derived from the coefficient
effect or differences in the response of policy-treated
watersheds compared to a set of control watersheds.

Environmental quality improvement happens for two main reasons: rising income levels and
the formulation of new environmental policies (Dasgupta et al., 2002; Grossman &
Krueger, 1995; Hu et al., 2022). The traditional idea of the income and pollution relationship
states that pollution increases as income increases, but pollution starts coming down after
income reaches a threshold or turning point (Paudel et al.,
2005). This relationship is based on
the assertion that as economic activities such as manufacturing, agriculture, and transportation
grow in the early stages of economic growth, they generate more pollution. However, as
incomes rise, people tend to demand cleaner environments, and also the economy becomes
more service-oriented. The shape of this relationship is often depicted as an inverted U-shaped
curve, with pollution increasing with economic growth until a turning point is reached, after
which pollution begins to decrease. The shape of the income and pollution relationship curve
varies based on pollutants, locations, and study periods (Lawell et al.,
2018).
The process that produces the inverted U-shaped relationship between pollution and
income is not linear, and technological change, education, public policy, and socioeconomic
conditions may eventually lead to a decline in pollution (Grossman & Krueger,
1995). Because
people tend to demand cleaner environments as income rises, governments implement policies
and regulations to curtail environmental degradation and promote environmentally friendly
practices. Some early studies on the relationship between pollution and income note that pollu-
tion has declined in rich as well as poor countries over time, and they credit government regula-
tion as partly responsible for this change (Dasgupta et al.,
2002). The formulation of
environmental policies by the government has been crucial in deterring the continuous rise of
pollution. There are many public policies that governments can employ in this regard. Early
regulations and standards established by the U.S. government to limit the amount of pollution
and other harmful activities that industries, businesses, and individuals can produce include
the Clean Air Act of 1963 and the Clean Water Act of 1972. Since then, many other laws and
regulations have been enacted at the federal and state levels to protect the environment further,
and this effort continues to the present day.
1

mental quality and that the relationship between income and pollution may vary across income
levels and policy contexts. These studies generally find that mandatory regulations, regular
inspections, strong enforcement mechanisms, and other incentive programs have significantly
improved environmental quality. For example, Culas (
2007) focuses on institutions for secure
property rights and better environmental policies on deforestation and finds that institutions
and policies can reduce the height of the inverted U-curve, meaning that environmental degra-
dation peaks at a lower level of income. Greenstone and Hanna (
2014) suggest that strict moni-
toring and enforcement of regulations is an effective way to reduce environmental pollution
and that environmental regulations are only effective if enforced properly. Shapiro and Walker
(
2018) find that environmental regulations were a significant factor in the reduction of

The objective of the present paper is to add to this strand of literature by studying the effec-
tiveness of the Mississippi River Basin Healthy Watersheds Initiative (MRBI) implemented by
the USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS). To the best of our knowledge, the
MRBI has not been studied for its effectiveness in reducing pollution. Our paper goes a step fur-
ther in distinguishing the specific impacts of income and policy on pollution reduction. We use
a decomposition method to identify the role of income and policy in reducing water pollution
in the United States. We use unique 12-digit hydrological unit code level data from 12 U.S.
states where the Mississippi River Basin Healthy Watershed Initiatives have been in operation
to establish the role of environmental policy and income in reducing pollution. We focus on
nitrogen levels in surface water as our primary pollution outcome variable of interest.
Our results show that the MRBI policy for the 2012 cohort of implemented watersheds had
a large and statistically significant effect on reducing water pollution. The treated watersheds
across five states (Arkansas, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, and Tennessee) saw a 43% reduction in
nitrogen concentrations when comparing the 2009 –2011 pre-treatment period to the 2012 –2018
post-treatment period. Watersheds in Tennessee saw the largest improvements. Treated water-
sheds in Arkansas also experienced a substantial decline in nitrogen levels of 19%. Some varia-
tions in the strength of the response across watersheds can occur. Decomposition results show
that 79% of the improvement in water quality from policy treatment is derived from an endow-
ment effect, driven mainly by location-fixed effects that include cross-sectional mean differ-
ences in income, among other characteristics. The effect of income on pollution outcomes is a
long-run phenomenon related to economic growth. Results show that 21% of differences are
derived from the coefficient effect or differences in the response of policy-treated watersheds
compared to a set of control watersheds. This result indicates that policy can have a substantial
effect, particularly for investments seeking immediate short-term gains.
First, we introduce a model that shows the interconnection of water pollution, environmen-
tal policy, and income. Second, we describe the Mississippi River Basin Healthy Watersheds Ini-
tiative that was launched by the United States Department of Agriculture to improve water

tures of the data. Fourth, we explain our difference-in-differences and decomposition empirical
approach, Results and conclusions are presented next. An extended theoretical model is shown
in the appendix.

MISSISSIPPI RIVER BASIN HEALTHY WATERSHEDS
INITIATIVE (MRBI)
The US Department of Agriculture (USDA) NRCS launched the MRBI in 2009 to improve water
quality health in the Mississippi River watersheds (US EPA, 2022). The initiative leverages the
2008 Farm Bill conservation programs funding, and the initiative was introduced to address ele-
vated nutrient levels in the Mississippi River, which flow downstream and contribute to the
Gulf of Mexico Hypoxic Zone.
The MRBI program supports and funds farm conservation investments designed to reduce
agricultural sources of nutrient and sediment pollution, as well as local water quality monitor-
ing and assessment resources in twelve states adjoining the Mississippi and Ohio rivers.
2 MRBI
is connected to and uses several Farm Bill programs for funding, most notably the Environmen-
tal Quality Incentives Program (EQIP) and the Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP). These
programs provide technical and financial assistance to agricultural producers and forest land-
owners to implement conservation practices to promote soil health, reduce erosion, and lessen
nutrient runoff. The program is a collaboration with state water quality agencies. MRBI funds
are allocated to states for use only within priority watersheds with approved Cooperative Con-
servation Partnership Initiative (CCPI) projects. NCRS State Conservationists solicit proposals
for potential partner institutions to nominate watershed projects at the 12-digit Hydrologic Unit

within a set of MRBI identified HUC8 Focus Watersheds representing high-priority areas. From

WATERSHED POLLUTION AND INCOME DATA
The primary data used for the paper include nitrogen pollution level readings at the monitoring
station linked to MRBI-approved HUC12 watersheds and county-level Gross Domestic Product
(GDP) data.
Water quality outcome data are sourced from the Water Quality Portal (WQP) of the
National Water Quality Monitoring Council, a cooperative service that consolidates data from
the US Geological Survey (USGS) National Water Information System (NWIS), Environmental
Protection Agency (EPA) STOrage and RETrieval (STORET) Data Warehouse, USDA Research
Service Sustaining The Earth's Watersheds-Agricultural Research Database System
(STEWARDS), and hundreds of federal, state, local and tribal agencies and monitoring organi-
zations. The data on nitrogen pollution levels are standardized and harmonized by Krasovich
et al. (
2022) into the Standardized Nitrogen and Phosphorus Dataset (SNAPD), where measure-
ments (in mg/L) can be compared across time and space for the Mississippi/Atchafalaya River
Basin. The SNAPD project solved issues of the comparability of water quality data from non-
point source (NPS) water pollution monitoring, particularly challenging due to decentralized
agricultural production across expansive land regions, the diffuse transport of contaminants
into waterways, and the numerous authorities and institutions performing the monitoring. The
data standardize the units of measurement (e.g., mg/L or ppm), the chemical form of the nutri-
ent (e.g., nitrate or nitrogen), and sample fraction (e.g., filtered or unfiltered) variations in sam-
ple reporting.

Decomposition
We use a Kitagawa-Oaxaca-Blinder (KOB) counterfactual decomposition method
(Blinder,
1973; Kitagawa, 1955; Oaxaca, 1973) to explore the mean nitrogen water quality out-
come differences between two groups of watersheds, specifically MRBI-treated and non-MRBI
watersheds for the twelve states of the Mississippi and Ohio River Basins. The procedure
decomposes mean differences into a coefficient effect and endowment effect. The coefficient
effect, also called the rate effect or unexplained difference, quantifies how much of the mean
difference can be attributable to the different treatment estimates (slope coefficients) holding
characteristics (covariates) constant between groups. The endowment effect, also called the dis-
tributional effect or explained difference, quantifies how much the mean difference is based on
the different explanatory covariates between the two groups.

To test if farmers in either the control or treatment watersheds changed their agricultural

We explore the extent to which our ATET estimates are robust to the inclusion of multiple
MRBI watershed program implementation years, specifically 2015 and 2016. Due to potential
econometric issues with staggered adoption and the interpretation of results, we use a synthetic
difference-in-differences (SDID) estimator (Arkhangelsky et al.,
2021; Clarke et al., 2023). Like
synthetic control methods (Abadie et al., 2010, 2015; Abadie & Gardeazabal, 2003), SDID forms
a unique convex weighting of underlying control units to create a synthetic control that is
closely matched to the treated unit in pre-treatment outcomes. This considerably loosens the
need for parallel trend assumptions. The ATET estimate using the staggered adoption design
for the 2012, 2015, and 2016 MRBI watersheds is a weighted estimand as described in
Arkhangelsky et al. (
2021). The principal disadvantage of the SDID implementation is a require-

the specifications in Table

the SDID ATET estimate is /C0 0.449 and significant (with standard error 0.198).

CONCLUSIONS
This paper presented evidence on the effect of policy and income on water pollution and found
the influence of both on outcomes. Taken together, the DID and decomposition evidence
showed a substantial impact of MRBI program implementation on nitrogen concentration
levels in the cohort of watersheds that participated in the 2012 MRBI program implementation
as well as later cohorts for 2015 and 2016. The 2012 estimated decrease of 0.75 mg/L is a sub-
stantial effect. However, given the focus of state regulators on degraded watersheds, some cau-
tion is warranted in interpreting the magnitude of the effects more broadly on all potential
watersheds that might participate in the program. Among priority HUC8 watersheds, substan-
tial variation in water quality exists across locations and time. State-specific results revealed het-
erogeneity in policy response. These are, in part, driven by idiosyncratic differences in weather
and voluntary participation at the farm level. DID results are all highly significant. More work
can be done to explore the effect of MRBI and other policy programs on watershed quality
improvements. The main issue centers on policy scale.
The MRBI program is a substantive and comprehensive commitment by federal, state, and
private partners to work with farmers to implement water quality improvement technologies.
However, future work might explore results for later periods and longer durations, the extent to
which knowledge about practice adoption, nutrient retention success, water quality, and farm-
land improvements can be disseminated to farmers in other watershed areas. In addition,
research on the longer-term persistence of land and water resilience outcomes is warranted.
Future research might explore both the interplay and interaction effects of policy and income
on water outcomes, as well as the dynamics of effects over time.
