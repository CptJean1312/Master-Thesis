1. Introduction
Flooding of coastal and ﬂuvial systems are the most signiﬁcant
natural hazards that modern society is subject to, affecting several
million people globally each year (Jonkman, 2005; UNISDR, 2009).
The total population and the economic value of material assets
located in zones prone to ﬂooding have increased dramatically
over the past decades and are expected to increase further due to:
(1) an overall growth in population and wealth; and (2) increases
in sea-level and ﬂood frequency due to climate change (IPCC, 2007;
UNISDR, 2011). Hence, it may be expected that global ﬂood risk
will increase over time (UNISDR, 2009, 2011), whereby ﬂood risk is
deﬁned as the product of hazard (the chance and characteristics of
a ﬂood event), exposure (the population and value of assets subject
to ﬂooding) and vulnerability (the capacity of a society to deal with
the event) (e.g. Kron, 2005).
The knowledge base with respect to global ﬂood risk is,
however, rather limited. Traditionally, river ﬂood risk assessments
have been limited to the local (e.g. Bouwer et al., 2010; Ward et al.,
2011) or national (e.g. de Moel et al., 2011; Hall et al., 2005) scales.
The potential ﬂood damage estimates in these risk assessments are
most commonly conducted by using land-use speciﬁc depth–
damage functions, i.e. relationships between ﬂood depth and
relative damage per area unit, in combination with land-cover data
of the ﬂooded area (Smith, 1994; Merz et al., 2010). However, the
application of methodologies used in regional studies at the global
scale has been difﬁcult. A particular problem has been modelling
the global hazard areas, i.e. the areas that would potentially be
inundated in the event of ﬂooding. This has been hampered by
signiﬁcant processing times and limited data availability, for
example on aspects such as river speciﬁc hydraulic information
and high-resolution elevation data (e.g. Woodhead et al., 2007;
Messner et al., 2007). Moreover, detailed land-use data are not
available on a global scale, and depth–damage functions vary
signiﬁcantly per country and study, both in terms of slope (the
increase of damage with depth) and maximum damage estimates
(total maximum loss per area unit) (Barredo and De Roo, 2010).
In spite of these limitations, recent research has simulated the
riverine ﬂood hazard area at the pan-European (Lugeri et al., 2006;

A B S T R A C T
Flood damage modelling has traditionally been limited to the local, regional or national scale. Recent
ﬂood events, population growth and climate change concerns have increased the need for global
methods with both spatial and temporal dynamics. This paper presents a ﬁrst estimation of global
economic exposure to both river and coastal ﬂooding for the period 1970–2050, using two different
methods for damage assessment. One method is based on population and the second is based on land-
use within areas subject to 1/100 year ﬂood events. On the basis of population density and GDP per
capita, we estimate a total global exposure to river and coastal ﬂooding of 46 trillion USD in 2010. By
2050, these numbers are projected to increase to 158 trillion USD. Using a land-use based assessment, we
estimated a total ﬂood exposure of 27 trillion USD in 2010. For 2050 we simulate a total exposure of 80
trillion USD. The largest absolute exposure changes between 1970 and 2050 are simulated in North
America and Asia. In relative terms we project the largest increases in North Africa and Sub-Saharan
Africa. The models also show systematically larger growth in the population living within hazard zones
compared to total population growth. While the methods unveil similar overall trends in ﬂood exposure,
there are signiﬁcant differences in the estimates and geographical distribution. These differences result
from inherent model characteristics and the varying relationship between population density and the
total urban area in the regions of analysis. We propose further research on the modelling of inundation
characteristics and ﬂood protection standards, which can complement the methodologies presented in
this paper to enable the development of a global ﬂood risk framework.

Studies on disaster risk at the global scale are important for
global development studies, for example for the estimation of the
costs associated with adapting to climate change. In recent years,
there has been increased attention to such studies (e.g. Ward et al.,
2010; The World Bank, 2009). However, studies at the global scale
in the water sector have been hampered by the lack of global
datasets and methods (e.g. Ward et al., 2010).
In this paper, we provide a ﬁrst exposure analysis that (1)
includes both river and coastal ﬂooding; (2) is on a global scale; (3)
applies two different methods for exposure analysis; and (4)
assesses exposure over several decades, spanning both past and
future. We perform our analyses on both historical and future
time-series of spatial exposure data for the period 1970–2050,
which will bring more insights into the spatiotemporal aspects of
global exposure to the two types of ﬂooding. The main aims of this
study are:
/C15  to develop and assess two distinct approaches for simulating
global ﬂood exposure;
/C15  to identify the main geographical areas exposed to ﬂooding and
the trends over time;
/C15  to assess the differences between the two approaches.
2. Methodology
In this paper, we apply two distinct methodologies  for
assessing ﬂood exposure on a global scale (Fig. 1). Both methods
combine global datasets of the ﬂood hazard, human settlement
and economic indices. One approach starts from population
density data and uses national income data to estimate the value
of assets in the hazard area. This approach is referred to as the
‘population  method’.  The second approach, the ‘land-use
method’,  starts from urban area data. The data and methods
used to compute ﬂood exposure will be detailed in the following
sections.
2.1. Data
2.1.1. Area boundaries
We modelled ﬂood exposure at the geographical scale of both
countries and Food Producing Units (FPUs). Country boundaries
data were taken from the GADM global administrative boundaries
database (Hijmans, 2011), which recognises 209 territories,
divided into 9 main regions (Antarctica, Asia, Australia, Caribbean,
Europe, Latin America, North America, North Africa and Sub-
Saharan Africa). Because the resolution of the land-use, population
and ﬂood extent datasets (see Sections 2.1.2 and 2.1.4) are
relatively coarse, we present most of the results on the basis of
these aggregated regions and FPUs rather than countries. The main
country-level results can be found in Appendix A.
FPUs are a hybrid between political and hydrological zones (Cai
and Rosegrant, 2002; Rosegrant et al., 2002; de Fraiture, 2007) and
therefore give a more detailed picture of river ﬂood exposure than
country boundaries, especially for larger countries. For this study
we used the version of the FPUs as described by Kummu et al.
(2010), which has been applied in earlier global level studies (e.g.
Ward et al., 2010).
2.1.2. River ﬂood extent dataset
A spatially referenced database of river ﬂood extents was
created by Herold and Mouton (2011) and is publicly available on
the PREVIEW Global Risk Data Platform (http://preview.grid.
unep.ch/). The database is developed from a combination of
hydrological modelling and historic ﬂood records of the Dartmouth
Flood Observatory (Brakenridge and Anderson, 2011). The data are
available as a raster GIS layer of horizontal ﬂood extents for return
periods up to 100 years; the resolution of the dataset is 3000 /C2  3000
(ca. 1 km /C2  1 km at the equator).
Herold and Mouton (2011) point out several limitations of their
database. For example, the river discharges used in the hydrologi-
cal model are statistical estimates based on observed monthly data
for a limited number of gauging stations, and can therefore deviate
from reality. Also it is noted that geographical characteristics, like
sudden elevation differences and forest cover, can lead to over and
underestimation of the ﬂoodplain. Furthermore, the effects of ﬂood
defences on inundation extent are not included in the database. For
countries that do have sufﬁcient protection against 1/100 year
river discharge, this assumption entails that some of the modelled
ﬂood-prone areas will in reality not be ﬂooded. This assumption
thus leads to an overestimation of potential inundation areas
(Herold et al., 2011; Pappenberger et al., 2012). Irrespective of

2.1.3. Coastal ﬂood extent dataset
We produced coastal ﬂood zone maps using sea level data from
the DIVA model (Vafeidis et al., 2008) and a digital elevation model
(DEM). The DIVA model provides expected surge heights for a 100
year return period for over 5000 different coastal segments, and
has been used as a tool for risk assessment on a European scale (e.g.
Hinkel et al., 2010). We then deﬁned ﬂood prone zones as coastal
areas with an elevation less than the 100 year surge height, and
with a direct connection to the sea. The elevation model used is the
near-global SRTM DEM, with a horizontal resolution of 300 /C2  300 (ca.
90 m /C2  90 m at the equator) (Jarvis et al., 2008). Where these data
are not available (above 60 degrees north and below 60 degrees
south, which includes parts of Scandinavia, northern Canada,
Alaska and northern Russia), the GTOPO30 DEM is used (USGS,
1996). The GTOPO30 has a horizontal resolution of 3000 /C2  3000 (ca.
1 km /C2  1 km at the equator).
For both coastal and river ﬂood areas we assumed that the
hazard area remains constant over time, which means that the
areas exposed to a 1/100 year ﬂood in 1970 are the same as those
exposed in 2050 (i.e. we do not account for climate change and/or
land subsidence). Given that climate change might have a
signiﬁcant effect on the frequency and severity of future ﬂood
events, this assumption could lead to an underestimation of future
ﬂood prone areas. Also, like with the river ﬂood extents, we did not
include the inﬂuence of ﬂood defences in the analysis, which leads
to an overestimation of ﬂood plains in areas where coastal ﬂood
protection is in place.
2.1.4. Population, land-use and socioeconomic data
The exposure analyses are based on current (2005) and
historic (1970–2000)  population density and land-use data from
the HYDE database (Klein Goldewijk et al., 2010). The population
density data are in the format of number of people per square
kilometre. The format of the land-use data used in the analysis is
urban area in km2 per grid cell. Both datasets have an original
resolution of 50 /C2  50 (ca. 10 /C2  10 km at the equator). We used GDP
per capita data for 1970–2009  from the Penn World Tables
(Heston et al., 2011). For comparison purposes, all GDP ﬁgures
are in 2005 international  US dollars on purchase power parity
(PPP) basis.
We estimated future population and assets by applying the
World Bank’s  population and GDP per capita projections (Hughes
et al., 2010) to the population data extracted from the HYDE
database. These GDP forecasts are computed by averaging the
results of ﬁve economic models – Hope (2003), Nordhaus (1999),
Tol (2007), IEA (2008) and EIA (2008). The resulting global real GDP
per capita ﬁgures are very close to the IPCC A2 SRES scenario (IPCC,
2000). The population projections are based on the Medium
Fertility projection in UN Population Division’s  2006 Revision
(United Nations, 2006).

2.2. Exposure estimation
2.2.1. Population method
Population exposed to river and coastal ﬂooding was calculated
by selecting the population living within the ﬂood hazard areas
and summing this for each country and FPU area. To estimate
exposed assets, we applied Eq. (2) (developed by Nicholls et al.
(2008, p.48)), in which Ea is exposed assets, Ep is exposed
population and GDPpercapita(PPP) is GDP per capita at purchase
power parity, as derived from the Penn World Tables (Heston et al.,
2011).
Ea ¼ Ep /C2  GDPpercapitaðpppÞ /C2  5 (2)
As follows from the formula, the value of exposed assets is a
function of the exposed population and GDP per capita, multiplied
by 5. This factor 5 is estimated on the basis of relative investment,
growth of the asset base and average asset life-time. It is assumed
that GDP per capita is homogenous within each country. In reality
there might be geographical variation in productivity, for example
between cities and rural areas (Dall’erba,  2005; Broersma and van
Dijk, 2005). The methodology as applied here should only be used
on larger scales (e.g. national and supra-national) and may lead to
substantial spatial errors on smaller scales (e.g. local and regional).
2.2.2. Land-use method
Land-use based ﬂood damage calculation is the most common
method for estimating ﬂood damage, and studies have been
conducted for a large range of countries and study areas (e.g. Ward
et al., 2011; de Moel et al., 2011; Herath, 2003; Lugeri et al., 2006).
In land-use based damage calculations the rationale is to analyse
the different types of land-use within the ﬂood plain, estimate the
maximum damage per unit or surface area, retrieve ﬂood depths
and apply a speciﬁc depth–damage function for each type of land-
use (e.g. Smith, 1994; Green et al., 2011). The maximum damage
ﬁgures represent the total value of assets that would be lost at a
deﬁned maximum inundation depth (generally 3–6 metres).
Depth–damage functions represent the vulnerability of the
respective land-use type, as the increase of damage from 0 to
100% of the maximum damage ﬁgure with increasing inundation
depth. Internationally, however, there is a strong variation in
depth–damage curves and maximum damage ﬁgures used.
Barredo and De Roo (2010) have shown that Europe currently
has 11 nationally accepted models. In the United States the US
Corps of Engineers applies different depth–damage curves to each
district or ﬂood site (Scawthorn et al., 2006). In developing
countries there are fewer well-developed ﬂood damage assess-
ment methodologies available, and the studies that have been
conducted are generally on a local scale (e.g. Yang and Tsai, 2000).
Each of the developed models has developed different estimations
of maximum damage (asset values) per square metres as well as
different depth–damage functions
Due to the lack of a globally applicable ﬂood damage model, we
have applied an existing national damage estimation method,
normalised to GDP per capita for each country. The maximum
damage ﬁgures are taken from the Damage Scanner (Klijn et al.,
2007), which is based on the HIS-SSM model, the standard method
for ﬂood damage simulation in the Netherlands (Kok et al., 2005).
Because ﬂood depth information is not available, we assessed ﬂood

3. Results
In this section, we discuss the results of the analysis as
described in the preceding part. Sections 3.1 and 3.2 present the
key outcomes regarding global exposure to river and coastal
ﬂooding, respectively. In Section 3.3, the river and coastal hazards
are combined in an assessment of total global exposure to 1/100
year ﬂoods. In Section 3.4 we discuss a concise analysis of ﬂood
exposure development in relation to general population growth.
3.1. Global exposure to river ﬂooding
3.1.1. Population method
3.1.1.1. Past and current exposure. Exposed assets to river ﬂooding
total 35 trillion (Fig. 2), up from 1.8 trillion USD in 1970. Overall,
approximately 48% of this increase can be subscribed to population
growth while the other 52% result from an increase in wealth. For
2010, the total global population exposed to a 1/100 year river
ﬂood amounts to 805 million, a ﬁgure approximately twice the
1970 level (Fig. 3a).
A signiﬁcant part, 73%, of the total population exposed to river
ﬂooding is living in Asia, followed by Europe and Sub-Saharan
Africa (Fig. 3a). The largest relative increase in exposed population
over the period 1970–2010 is simulated in Sub-Saharan Africa
(188%) and North Africa (132%). Sub-Saharan Africa is particularly
characterised by a more than linear increase in population
exposure, while on the other hand Europe experiences a
stabilisation in total and exposed population.
In spite of a relatively low average GDP per capita, the region
with the largest total value of assets in river ﬂood hazard areas is
Asia (17 trillion USD), followed by Europe and North America
(Fig. 3b). Over the past 40 years all regions have experienced strong
growth in the value of assets exposed to river ﬂooding. The largest
relative increase over this period is simulated in Asia (4300%) and
North Africa (2900%).
These rather substantial relative increases result from simulta-
neous strong growth in both GDP per capita and population
between 1970 and 2010, averaging 1163% and 92% respectively in
Asia. Also reﬂected in the results is the impact of the 2007–2009
ﬁnancial crisis (Fig. 3b). GDP per capita in all regions dropped in
those years, most profoundly in western economies. Since the
applied methodology for measuring exposure is a direct function of
GDP per capita, ﬂuctuations in GDP per capita are directly
translated into changes in the value of exposed assets. In reality,
this relationship may not be linear or have a time lag, and exposure
of assets may remain constant while GDP declines. The limitations
of the methodologies will be discussed in more detail in Section 4.
3.2. Future exposure
The analyses show an overall increase in population and asset
exposure over the next decades. In 2050 the total population
exposed to 1/100 river ﬂoods is simulated to reach 1.05 billion, a
31% increase from current levels. The total exposed assets will
amount to 126 trillion, up 250% from 2010.
In relative terms, exposed population growth is projected to be
highest in Sub-Saharan Africa (104%) (Fig. 3a). The stabilising trend
observed in Europe over the period 1970–2010 turns into a decline
in the subsequent 40 years – by 2050 the exposed population is
projected to have fallen by 13%. As a result of conjunct population
and GDP per capita growth, relative exposed assets increases are
signiﬁcantly higher – for 75 of the countries included in the
analysis a growth of more than 300% is simulated. Overall, the
largest growth of economic exposure is in Asia (370%), followed by
Sub-Saharan and North Africa. In spite of a fall in exposed
population, the value of assets in Europe’s  river ﬂood zones is still
growing by an estimated 127%.

3.3. Future exposure
The simulation results show a continuation of the upward trend
in urban area exposure for the period 2010–2050. Similar to
population exposure, this trend is generally slowing in North
America, Europe, Australia and Latin America, and increasing in the
developing regions (Fig. 3c). In the year 2050, the total urban area
exposed to 1/100 river ﬂoods will have reached an approximate
49,000 km2, an increase of 61% from the 2010 levels. The resulting
maximum damages will increase to 63 billion USD for river
ﬂooding, up 200% from the 2010 estimates. This ﬁgure is small
compared to the 162 trillion USD estimate according to the
population method.
For 2050, the maximum damage in North America is estimated
at 27 trillion USD, followed by Asia (20 trillion USD) (Fig. 3d). Again,
the results show a higher relative increase in exposed urban area
than exposed population between 2010 and 2050. The largest
relative increase is simulated in Sub-Saharan Africa (220%) and
North Africa (104%). Similarly, the relative growth of economic
exposure according to the land-use method is higher than the
relative growth in exposed assets following the population
method. Asia (562%) and Sub-Saharan Africa (544%) are simulated
to experience the strongest relative increase.

3.5. Future exposure
In the year 2050 an estimated 345 million people will be living
in 1/100 coastal ﬂood hazard areas; this is 25% more than in 2010.
The corresponding value of assets is USD 43 trillion, an increase of
approximately 200% from 2010. In absolute numbers, the largest
increase of population in these areas is projected in Asia (the
country results in Appendix A show that in Bangladesh alone, an
additional 26 million people are simulated to be living in coastal
hazard zones by 2050). Relative to current levels, exposed
population is projected to see the strongest growth in Sub-Saharan
and North Africa. Whereas the population exposed to river ﬂooding
in Europe is simulated to fall by 13% between 2010 and 2050, the
coastal population exposure increases by 2%. This is in spite of an
expected decrease in total population of 9%, which indicates that
on a European level coastal ﬂood hazard zones are characterised by
higher than average population growth. The largest total increases
in the value of assets in coastal hazard zones until 2050 are
simulated in Asia (20 trillion USD) and Europe. In terms of relative
exposure increase, the accent shifts to Sub-Saharan and North
Africa (both approximately 330%).
3.5.1. Land-use method
3.5.1.1. Past and current exposure. In 2010, the total urban area
within 1/100 coastal ﬂood hazard zones was 12,000 km2, with a
corresponding maximum damage of USD 7.8 trillion. In 1970 this
ﬁgure was only 1.5 trillion. The regional distribution of coastal
exposure is similar to the land-use based estimates for river
ﬂooding. In 2010, Asia (5000 km2), Europe (2300 km2) and North
America (1900 km2) have the largest exposed urban surface area in
coastal hazard zones (Fig. 4c). Due to differences in GDP per capita,
the resulting monetary exposure is in reverse order – North
America (1.6 trillion USD), followed by Europe (1.5 trillion USD)
and Asia (1 trillion USD) (Fig. 4d). Again, the largest relative
increase in exposed urban area between 1970 and 2010 occurred in
Sub-Saharan Africa (878%) and North Africa (278%). In contrast to
river ﬂooding, the largest relative change in monetary exposure is
also simulated in these areas, 767% and 791% respectively, with
Asia ranking only third.

3.7. Total ﬂood exposure
Because a signiﬁcant part of the global population lives in river
deltas, areas typically threatened by both river and coastal
ﬂooding, we expect an overlap in the population exposed to either
of these hazards. The total population and assets exposed to 1/100
year ﬂooding are less than the sums of river and coastal ﬂooding.
By combining the different ﬂood hazard zones used in this study,
assessing the overlapping areas, and applying the same exposure
calculation methods as described previously, we have estimated
the total global exposure to both 1/100 river- and coastal ﬂoods.
3.7.1. Population method
Globally, the total population exposed to this combined ﬂood
hazard is 992 million in 2010, with a total assets value of 46 trillion
USD. In 1970, 523 million people and assets amounting to 2.5
trillion USD were located in the same areas. By the year 2050 we
project approximately 1.3 billion people in ﬂood hazard zones,
with a total value of exposed assets of 158 billion USD.
The largest numbers of people living in the combined hazard
zones are currently found in Asia (716 million), Europe (89
million) and Sub-Saharan Africa (67 million). On a national scale,
we see several countries that have large numbers of people
exposed to both river and coastal ﬂooding.  For both hazards
individually,  Bangladesh, China, India and Vietnam have the 4
largest exposed populations (although the order is different for
river and coastal ﬂooding).  Not surprisingly,  they also rank highly
on the combined exposure measure, accounting for over 560
million persons, or almost 60% of the world total (see
Appendix A).
The highest total value of assets exposed to 1/100 year ﬂoods is
also located in Asia (6.9 trillion USD), followed by Europe (3.9
trillion USD) and North America (1.4 trillion USD). Apart from the
visible effect of the recent ﬁnancial recession in the exposure
results, the value of exposed assets has been increasing more than
linearly in all regions over the past 40 years and is projected to do
so until 2050.
As expected, the total number of people and value of assets
located in ﬂood hazard zones are less than the sums of coastal and
river exposure – the overlap is about 8% for population exposure
and 4% for asset exposure in 2010. The region with the largest
overlap in both exposed population and exposed assets is Asia,
with 9% and 7% respectively; North America follows with
approximately 6%. Sub-Saharan Africa, the Caribbean and
Australia, on the other hand, are characterised by a small value
of approximately 1%. Visual inspection of the data shows that the
overlapping areas are located in areas of low elevation around river
deltas. The people and assets located in these regions are
confronted with two separate hazards and are, keeping everything
else constant, at a higher risk of ﬂooding.
3.7.2. Land-use method
Approximately 44,000 km2 of urban area is exposed to either
coastal or river ﬂooding in 2010, up from 18,000 km2 in 1970. For
the year 2050, our simulations show a total urban surface of
72,000 km2 located in ﬂood zones, causing monetary exposure to
rise from 27 trillion USD in 2010 to 80 trillion USD by 2050. Thus,
for the current situation, the land-use based estimate is 70% lower
than the population based estimate.
In 2010, the largest urban area in the combined ﬂood hazard
zones are found in Asia (17,000 km2), North America (11,000 km2)
and Europe (8000 km2). Again, the monetary exposures are ranked
differently, with North America being the most exposed region (13
trillion USD), followed by Europe (6.1 trillion USD) and Asia (4.2
trillion USD). These estimates show a large different to those
resulting from the population method; see Section 4.2 for a
discussion of this discrepancy.
The difference between the calculated total ﬂood exposure and
the sum of river and coastal ﬂooding is similar whether we use the
land-use method or the population method. Again, Asia and North
America have relatively large areas exposed to both hazards, 7%
and 6% respectively, while the Caribbean, Australia and Sub-
Saharan Africa have a relatively small overlap.

4.1. Comparison of results between methods
While the estimates of global exposure to river and coastal
ﬂooding show similar global trends over time, there are signiﬁcant
differences between the two damage estimation methods used in
this analysis. On a global level, the population method gives a
higher estimation than the land-use method. The difference is 71%
in 2010 and increases to 98% in 2050. Also, the regional distribution
of exposure varies signiﬁcantly between the two approaches.
While the population method consistently ranks Asia as having the
highest value of exposed assets, on the basis of land-use the
highest exposed asset values are found in the United States and
Europe. The differences arise from important differences between
the two approaches.
The ﬁrst important difference is the fact that population does
not necessarily go hand in hand with urban land-use – rural
societies will therefore have a lower damage estimation based on
land-use than based on population. Also, for economic and political
reasons, population growth does not lead to an increase of urban
area at the same degree in every region (Brueckner, 2000).
Second, it should be noted that the population based method is
a maximum exposure measure, based on the long-term value of
assets, while the land-use method is designed to estimate actual
maximum damages based on the estimated replacement value of
urban area. For any given country the gap between the two
methods seems to increase with growing GDP per capita, with the
population based estimation being the higher of the two.
Finally, it is important to acknowledge the generalisations
made in both methods. In the population based method it is
assumed that the value of assets, based on GDP per capita, is
homogenous within each country. The land-use based method
assumes a uniform value of all exposed urban area of that country.
In reality, however, both the value of assets and the damage to
urban fabric varies strongly with, amongst other factors, economic
activity and land-use function (e.g. Merz et al., 2010).
The differences between the methods are also reﬂected in the
results and the country rankings of the two estimations. The
United States, for example, are ranked 5th in per capita river
ﬂooding exposure according to the population based method, but
have the highest exposure according to the land-use method.
China, on the other hand, has the highest total exposed assets
ranking in the population based estimation, but ranks third under
the land-use method (see Appendix A). The reason for the
divergence is the regional variation in exposed population/exposed
urban area ratios (Table 2). In 1970, these ratios range from an
average 3529 exposed people per exposed urban km2 in Australia
to more than 363,000 in Sub-Saharan Africa. For the United States,
the average number of exposed persons per exposed urban km2 in
the year 2010 is 2754, while the ﬁgure for China is 37,131,
reﬂecting signiﬁcant differences in the degree of urbanisation. This
illustrates the general ﬁnding that, according to the data used in
this study, developed countries show a larger urban surface area
per inhabitant. All regions show an increase in the amount of
exposed urban area per exposed person, indicating a stronger
growth of city areas relative to their population in ﬂood-prone
areas. Table 2 shows that this trend is stronger in the developing
than in developed regions, which means there is a trend of
convergence. This ﬁnding corresponds to empirical studies in the
United States (Brueckner and Fansler, 1983) and Europe (Kasanko
et al., 2006), who also show that the total urban surface area is an
increasing function of population and income. The changing ratio
of population over urban area also means that the difference
between the estimated ﬂood exposure resulting from the
population method and land-use method is changing. With the
regional differences in urbanisation remaining, however, this
should be taken into account when selecting an exposure
assessment method.
From these results it is difﬁcult to say which of the two
approaches is better suited for estimating ﬂood exposure on a
global scale. In general it can be argued that land-use data of the
actual assets being ﬂooded is the most accurate measure of
damage, which is why land-use based assessment is the preferred
approach in small-scale studies. On a global scale, however, land-
use types are generalised, with only one class of urban land cover

be expected to vary signiﬁcantly within any given country (e.g.
Rice et al., 2006; Escobal and Torero, 2005). Apart from these
issues, however, population can be argued to be more homogenous
across different countries than urban build-up, and might
therefore be a better proxy for global ﬂood exposure. More
research is required to assess whether this is indeed the case.
4.2. Comparison with other studies
The results presented here support some important ﬁndings
from previous studies. Similar to the observations of the IPCC
(2007), we ﬁnd a relatively strong increase of physical and
monetary exposure to ﬂoods in developing countries, even without
accounting for climate change. The United Nations (UNISDR, 2011)
recently showed that the exposure of economic assets to river
ﬂooding is increasing in all regions, while the mortality risk in
developed countries is declining due to increasing income and
better governance (e.g. emergency planning and preparedness).
Although our methodologies do not account for governance effects,
the ﬁndings do show a decline in exposed population in Europe and
a strong increase in Africa and Asia, while the value of exposed
assets is rising consistently in all areas. With respect to coastal
ﬂood risk, our ﬁnding that the population growth in ﬂood prone
areas is higher than average population growth corresponds to the
results of Small and Nicholls (2003), who also ﬁnd this relative
concentration of people in coastal zones. Also, the strong
concentration and increase of urban area in coastal ﬂood plains
that we present in this paper is consistent with the relatively
strong growth of coastal cities found by Nicholls (1995) and
Nicholls et al. (2008).
Quantitatively, our total river ﬂooding exposure estimates (ca
35 trillion USD in 2010) are close to earlier projections by the
United Nations (2009) (ca 40 trillion USD), who use the same ﬂood
extent data but different GDP and population data. At the same
time our ﬁgures of economic exposure to coastal ﬂooding are a
factor 3 higher than the global results of Nicholls et al. (2008). The
reason is that Nicholls et al., although applying the same
population-based exposure equation, only includes port cities
and does not include rural areas and smaller urban regions in his
assessment. The two studies are also based on different population
density databases.
4.3. Limitations and future research
There are several limitations intrinsic to the global analysis
presented in this paper. In the ﬁrst place, we have assumed the
river and coastal ﬂood hazard zones to be constant over time.
While it is possible to correct the coastal ﬂood hazard for climate
change, land subsidence or (changes in) ﬂood protection for a
selection of areas (see Nicholls et al., 2008), the necessary data is
not available on a global scale. Future studies should aim to create
dynamic estimates of changing ﬂood extent and depth based on
projected future climate scenarios, and including information on
protection standards. The latter could be achieved by introducing a
corresponding variable in the risk analysis based on, for example,
GDP and government spending on infrastructure.
Furthermore, both damage estimation methods have several
limitations. The population based method is based upon a simple
linear relation with GDP that is assumed to be constant over time
and space, and which might actually be more complex in reality.
The land-use approach is based on one urban land-use class and
maximum damage ﬁgure for all urban areas, adjusted to GDP.
Again, reality is more complicated, with maximum damage ﬁgures
varying signiﬁcantly within urban settlements (Kok et al., 2005; de
Moel et al., 2011; Scawthorn et al., 2006). However, the global data
currently available over a long period of time do not allow for a
more detailed analysis.
In this study we applied the Damage Scanner’s  maximum
damage per m2 estimate (Klijn et al., 2007), normalised to GDP per
capita for 2010. The choice of this maximum damage value,
compared to maximum damage values used in four other ﬂood
damage models, is assessed in a sensitivity analysis. The maximum
damage per square metre estimates derived from the different
models for the year 2010 are shown in Fig. 6. More details can be
found in Table A.1 in Appendix A. The Multi-coloured Manual
(United Kingdom) (Penning-Rowsell et al., 2010) and Rhine Atlas
(Rhine basin) (IKSR, 2001) are based on market values of exposed
assets. The NHK2000 model (Germany) (BVBW, 2001), Damage
Scanner (Netherlands) (Kok et al., 2005) and Flemish method
(Belgium) (Vanneuville et al., 2006) are based on replacement
values, which are generally higher than market values (De Moel
and Aerts, 2011). Overall the maximum damage estimate from the
Damage Scanner model is in the middle of the values from the
other models in this comparison. Future studies, however, should
aim at aggregating different national methodologies and develop-
ing an internationally adjusted model. A ﬁrst attempt at gathering
and aggregating models on a European scale has been carried out
by Barredo and De Roo (2010). Further research is required to link
damage estimates across a wider range of countries and
continents.
Finally, we recall the relationship risk = hazard /C2  exposure /C2
vulnerability. In both the population and the land-use method, the
exposure estimates are directly and positively related to GDP per
capita. The rationale behind this assumption is that the direct and
indirect economic value of persons and assets is higher in high-
income than in low-income countries (UNISDR, 2011). In reality,
however, the relationship between GDP and ﬂood exposure is more
complex. In the analysis, countries with high additional national
income from natural resources, like various regions in the Middle-
East, are assigned very high exposure estimates. In fact, however,
the high overall GDP ﬁgure might not be directly related to the
actual productivity of the population exposed to ﬂooding. Rather,
the main sources of income are likely to be focussed on several
speciﬁc sites or regions (Hill, 2000). Furthermore, high-income

5. Conclusions
We have applied two distinctive methods for deriving ﬂood
exposure in order to estimate historical, current and future ﬂood
risk on a global scale. Both approaches start from historical
spatial datasets, whereby one is based on population density
and the other on the extent of urban area within ﬂood hazard
zones. There is already an awareness that global ﬂood risk is
increasing over time and that the changes taking place are not
uniform over different geographical areas. Our quantitative
analysis on a global scale over a longer time period provides
insights in the trends and can support international  decision
making on land use, climate change and disaster relief policies.
Furthermore,  the results of our study are in line with the trend
of increasing global aggregation of natural disaster knowledge.
In 2005, the United Nations implemented the Hyogo Framework
for Action (HFA), a framework for international  collaboration  on
the reduction of natural disaster risk (UNISDR, 2005). In their
2009 assessment of global disaster risk, the United Nations
(UNISDR, 2009) reinforce their call for further harmonisation  of
natural hazards research.
For 2010, we ﬁnd global population totals exposed to ﬂooding of
992 million with corresponding value of assets of 46 trillion USD.
The highest numbers of people exposed to river and coastal
ﬂooding are generally found in Asian countries. In contrast, the
highest values of exposed assets are commonly found in developed
regions. The total area of urban land-cover in ﬂood zones is
estimated at 44,000 km2 in 2010, resulting in an approximate land-
use based exposure ﬁgure of 18.3 trillion USD. Both the results of
the period 1970–2010 and the projections for 2010–2050 show an
increase in exposure to river and coastal ﬂooding. The strongest
increases are in those regions that experience high GDP and
population growth over time. For 1970–2010 these regions are
generally located in Asia and the Middle-East. In the next four
decades, the largest relative exposure increases are expected in
Sub-Saharan Africa, both on the basis of the population and land-
use method.
In this study we compared two methods for damage estimation
and found important differences in the results. Because population
and urbanisation do not always coincide, rural communities
generally have a higher population based damage estimate,
compared to the land-use based estimate. We stress that the
available global land-use data are highly generalised, with only one
class for urban area. This raises questions about the homogeneity
of people and income versus buildings and their corresponding
value. The assumption of spatial homogeneity of productivity leads
to the apparent overestimation of damage for countries with high
income from natural resources. Neglecting differentiation in urban
land-use disregards variation in building style and economic
activity.
We highlight that more research is needed on several aspects of
the exposure methodologies presented here. Future studies should
focus on modelling actual ﬂood depths and more detailed land-use
data on a global scale, which would support the application of a
differentiated depth–damage model. Also, further work is needed
on international normalisation of damages, using other indicators
in addition to GDP per capita. Finally, more data is required on
current ﬂood protection standards on river and coastal segments
around the world. Combined with the methodologies presented in
this paper, this would enable the development of a global
framework for ﬂood risk assessment.
