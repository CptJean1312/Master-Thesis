Abstract
Up to now, it has been common practice to use technical ﬂood protection mea-
sures such as dikes and retention basins to secure the prosperity and well-being of
societies that are threatened by ﬂoods. Recent ﬂood events in Europe have demon-
strated in a profound way that extreme ﬂoods not only have the potential to cause
enormous damage but also that technical structures do not entirely solve the prob-
lem; additional concepts now need to be considered. During the Elbe ﬂood disas-
ter of 2013 in Germany, two dike breaks occurred. The unpredicted ﬂooding that
followed resulted in enormous harm and tremendous material damage to thou-
sands of inhabitants. Post-crisis assessment indicates that consideration of extreme
ﬂood events is essential to ﬂood risk management and that preparation is neces-
sary to mitigate negative consequences. The use of ﬂoodplain areas that lie close
to rivers is a key aspect in this respect. Even if dikes currently protect these areas,
a broad societal discussion must take place in order to optimise ﬂood prevention.
Introduction

inﬁltration capacity is essential for any amelioration of ﬂood-
ing. During ﬂoods, land functions as a temporary water stor-
age mechanism. Major ﬂoods are considered spatial events
because they usually cause large-scale inundation.
In ancient times, people usually settled away from hazardous
areas and tried to avoid the threat of ﬂooding. On the Elbe
River, settlement historically focussed on hilly areas close to the
river, as in the city of Magdeburg. This ancient city surround-
ing a famous cathedral is located more than 15 m above the
River’s mean water level (IKSE (Internationale Kommission
zum Schutz der Elbe), 2005). Signi ﬁcantly, city planning in
ancient times usually included ﬂood prevention measures.
Since no expensive or sophisticated hydraulic engineering
methods were then available, preparedness and prevention
were the methods of choice.
Owing to population growth, pressure to expand human
habitation on ﬂoodplains increased. Technical measures
became standard strategies for trying to protect land for settle-
ment and agriculture. Hydraulic engineering structures such as
dikes and retention basins became part of ‘engineered rivers’
(see e.g. Blackbourn, 2006). While in the Middle Ages, hydrau-
lic engineering structures such as dikes usually protected single
settlements and commercial facilities like mills, nowadays in
most developed countries, ﬂood protection measures have
evolved to constitute a complex ﬂood protection system along
a river or within a catchment area (Patt and Jüpner, 2013). The
Elbe River in Germany is a well-known example of this type of
development. Today, more than 500 km of the Elbe River are
nearly completely bordered by dikes.
As a result of these hydraulic engineering measures com-
pleted in recent centuries, it is estimated that today more
than 83% of the natural ﬂoodplains along the Elbe have
been eliminated (IKSE (Internationale Kommission zum
Schutz der Elbe), 2005). As a consequence, ﬂoods have
become more powerful, and their velocity as well as their
maximum discharge is generally increasing (BfG
(Bundesanstalt für Gewässerkunde), 2013). During the past
two decades, inhabitants along the Elbe River have suffered
from three large ﬂood events (2002, 2006, and 2013) and,
as a result, discussions about the correct approach to ﬂood
risk management are much more intensive than before.

effect for land use

These areas can be identi ﬁed by hydrodynamic model-
ling. Inundated areas on ﬂood hazard maps are indicated by
different colours. The darker the colour, the more extensive
the area inundated during a ﬂood event. With respect to rel-
evant European rivers, this is new information, and a clear
indication of the negative effects brought by a major ﬂood
for every single landmark near a river (Figure 1
). It clearly
indicates that the negative impact occurs not only naturally
on the ﬂoodplains themselves, but also in the hinterlands.
The extent of impact depends on the safety of ﬂood protec-
tion structures.
For much of history, installation of technical protective
structures produced a ‘sense of security ’ for people settling
behind dikes. Responsible local authorities and engineers
rarely informed inhabitants of the affected areas about
existing risks. As a result, people tended to abandon pre-
paredness and prevention measures against ﬂoods as soon
as technical protection measures were put in place
(Hartmann and Jüpner, 2014). People assumed that pro-
tection meant protection against all ﬂood events, and
naively believed in the ‘security promise ’ of technical pro-
tection measures. This phenomenon is known as the dike
paradox, and is compellingly described, for example, by
Hartmann (2011).
However, the reality is different: with increasing invest-
ment in technical protective measures, the potential for
damage increases as well (Munich Re, 2014). The incidence
of devastating damage escalates because people in the
affected areas rarely invest in any prevention measures and
were therefore unprepared for extreme ﬂoods. The overall
vulnerability of the ‘protected hinterland ’ is therefore
increasing and, as a result, for many communities the situa-
tion today is worse than ever.

for example as a result of dike failure – discussion about
appropriate prevention strategies resumes. In Germany,
such discussions began immediately after the catastrophic

Experiences from the recent ﬂood
disaster on the Elbe River in Germany
in 2013
A closer look at the 2013 ﬂood and some important lessons
it taught may lead to a better understanding of this ongoing
discussion. In June 2013, the catchment areas of the Elbe
and the upper Danube Rivers were impacted by a devastat-
ing ﬂood. Flooding was caused by heavy rainfall in Central
Europe, especially in the Southern and the Eastern parts of
Germany, between May 17 and June 2. The enormous rain-
fall in ﬁltrated the soil. At the end of May 2013, soil mois-
ture levels in nearly 40% of Germany were at the highest
levels ever recorded since the beginning of continuous
measurement in 1962 (BfG, 2013). Considering ongoing
discussions about the sources of extreme ﬂood events and
limits in ﬂood protection (Schanze, 2012; Birkmann et al .,
2015), this is remarkable.
Rainfall in combination with high pre-existing soil mois-
ture led to extreme ﬂooding in Germany. As a result, ﬂood-
ing surpassed the previous highest ﬂood levels over a
distance of more than 250 km (around 155 miles) along
the central Elbe and the lower Saale Rivers (Figure 2) (BfG,
2013; LHW (Landesbetrieb für Hochwasserschutz und
Wasserwirtschaft Sachsen-Anhalt), 2014).
In the central portion of the Elbe River in Germany, two
catastrophic dike breaks occurred nearly simultaneously dur-
ing peak discharge (Figure 2). On June 10, 2013 at around
midnight, a breach developed in the dike along the Elbe close
to the village of Fischbeck and expanded to a ﬁnal width of
90 m. Although a speci ﬁc cause for the dike failure was not
identiﬁed, it appears likely that it was due to soil instability
induced by internal erosion that led to slippage of both slopes
of the dike (Figure 3). Even though substantial emergency
action was taken to protect the integrity of the dike, over ﬂow
of the affected section could not be prevented (Figure 4).
The area between Elbe and Havel Rivers that was inun-
dated by the resulting breach is a relatively ﬂat valley formed
during the last glacial period. Flood water travelled parallel
to the Elbe for roughly 30 km following the slope of this
inland valley. The huge volume of water ﬂowing through the
breach in the dike (more than 40 million m
3 per day)

During the entire 2013 Elbe River ﬂood in Germany,
13 dike breaks were recorded along the Elbe River and its
major tributaries (Heyer and Stamm, 2013). This attests to
the necessity of engaging in a fundamental discussion about
sustainable ﬂood risk management that includes considera-
tion of the fact that catastrophic consequences can result
from the failure of hydraulic engineering structures con-
structed according to current building codes.

Public response and national ﬂood
protection programme
In Germany, but also in other European countries, the
2013 Elbe River ﬂood led to signi ﬁcant public discussion.
This was not limited to details about hydraulic engineering
but focused on appropriate strategies for ﬂood risk manage-
ment and the investment/resources required to prevent
future catastrophes.
In addition to a nation-wide programme of recovery
and rebuilding following the 2013 ﬂood, the federal state
accepted responsibility, for the ﬁrst time in the history of
modern Germany, for giving ﬁnancial support to German
states in their efforts to implement ﬂood protection mea-
sures as a matter of ‘nation-wide importance ’. As a conse-
quence, a ‘National Flood Protection Program ’ (Nationales
Hochwasserschutzprogramm) was established. Although
the German states had requested 5.4 billion euros, the fed-
eral government contributed only 1.2 billion euros over
the subsequent 10 years (LAWA (Bund/Länder-
Arbeitsgemeinschaft Wasser), 2014). Unfortunately, the
programme focuses only on storage polders and the reloca-
tion of dikes to increase water retention space. Referring to
the amount of ‘land’ required for insuring millions of cubic
metres of retention volume to store potential ﬂood water,
current discussion focuses mainly on the question of how
to obtain this land. To obtain usually means to buy land
and to use it for the purpose of storing water in case of a
major ﬂood. There are no available statutory programmes
in Germany, but federal programmes do allow responsible
authorities (mostly at state level) to use ﬁnancial resources
from public budgets. This is typically the case as long as
other aspects, such as ecological improvements, are part of
the measure.
An interesting new aspect in ﬂood risk management
includes ‘extreme ﬂood events ’. This mode of expression
is part of the EU ’s Floods Directive (EU, 2007) and is
now a legal term. Certainly, it does not make sense to
consider the occurrence of a Biblical Deluge, but it is
essential to consider the possibility that hydraulically
engineered structures might fail if design levels are
reached and surpassed. This would be a better and more
of realistic way of considering ‘extreme event scenarios ’
and should be incorporated into hydraulic engineering
practice.
An ongoing discussion of these issues in the ﬂood risk
management community has led to several interesting new
approaches. The Bavarian Water Management Authority
describes a system of resilient facilities that could cope
with rare ﬂood events (Figure 7). The notion of a resilient
system would still include technical structures such as
storage polders, but could easily be transferred to a system
in which the dike line is not of constant height, but rather
incorporates over ﬂow sections or hydraulic engineering
structures for controlling the ﬂow into the polders (see the
part ‘resilient system ’
). Thus during an extreme ﬂood,
water would over ﬂow the dike at predetermined locations
where the inundation of the hinterland will not have dis-
astrous results (BStMUV (Bayerisches Staatsministerium
für Umwelt und Verbraucherschutz), 2015). This strategy
contrasts with the existing ‘classical system ’, conceived to
address a single situation: the design ﬂood. If water levels
exceed the design water level and water begins over ﬂowing
the dike, there is no strategy in place for controlling the
situation. As a result, rural or urban areas could suddenly
and catastrophically be affected.
Discussion
What might be appropriate middle- and long-term strate-
gies in ﬂood risk management for reducing the potential
damage in our modern industrial society? A few strategies
should, I think, be pursued more comprehensively in order
to reduce disaster risk.
Consideration of extreme events
As mentioned above, failure of hydraulic engineering
structures sometimes occurs during major ﬂoods.
A responsible and comprehensive strategy must consider
this possibility. Even if ‘extreme event scenarios ’
(as de ﬁned in the EU Floods Directive) seldom occur,
estimated damage from such events is so enormous that
it must be considered. A responsible ﬂood risk manage-
ment strategy for a catchment area or a community must
take into consideration the disastrous effects of a failure
in the ﬂood protection system. Consequently, a substan-
tiated analysis of possible effects must focus on critical
infrastructures and assess their vulnerability (Gall and
Jüpner, 2015).

It is a matter of fact that the design level for technical ﬂood
protection measures depends on the value of the protected

The overall concept of ﬂood risk management should,
ﬁnally, lead to a more resilient society. This is the subject of
applied research; see for example, the Resilient Cities Proj-
ect (www.Resilient-cities.iclei.org). Flooding is usually only
one aspect considered along with others such as climate
change and drought.
Awareness and acceptance of the affected
inhabitants in such situations is essential
Common sense indicates that ef ﬁcient ﬂood risk manage-
ment must include structural as well as non-structural mea-
sures. Prevention is the key to reducing the negative impact
of ﬂooding. But no measure leads to complete security and
a 100% protection level. A certain degree of risk will always
remain.
Experiences from recent ﬂoods in Central Europe indi-
cate that the awareness and acceptance of the affected pop-
ulation is crucial for the ef ﬁcient reduction of potential
damage. A number of ﬂood prevention measures require
the availability of land to use as a retention area. This often
leads to a con ﬂict with landowners or land users, but judi-
cious economic incentives can help improve landowner/
user acceptance of ﬂood prevention measures (Hartmann,
2011). The European Floods Directive has therefore estab-
lished a legal framework that includes the potentially
affected inhabitants in creating ﬂood risk management
plans (EU, 2007).
Spatial planning: how to make land usable for
temporary water storage?
During a major ﬂood, storage polders function as ef ﬁcient
technical structures for reducing peak discharge (Patt and
Jüpner, 2013). They do not constitute the entire solution to
ﬂood problems, but represent an important complement to
other measures in the mitigation of catastrophic ﬂood
situations, structural as well as non-structural. From a
hydraulic engineering perspective, the capacity to tempo-
rarily store water before uncontrolled ﬂoodwaters inundate
an area of high economic value and threaten the life of the
inhabitants would be extremely helpful. But on the other
hand, such events are rare. For example, the biggest polder

It appears to me that the sensible approach for a sustain-
able ﬂood risk management strategy should be based on
preparedness for natural hazards and appropriate use of
land. Planners should be thinking about ways to convert
conventional systems of land use to more resilient ones.
This could include the temporary storage of ﬂoodwater in
public places or in areas used for agriculture. Both eco-
nomic and technical impediments need to be discussed and
solved in order to devise successful ﬂood risk management
strategies. In addition, existing technical structures such as
roads or parking lots that can be used to store or bypass
ﬂoodwater should be further developed. Synergistic possibi-
lities with climate adaptation measures should also be care-
fully investigated. They can undoubtedly contribute to a
more ﬂood-resilient society. Some helpful ideas have already
been tested and it is vitally important to continue this dis-
cussion among relevant disciplines in order to reach a more
efﬁcient approach to ﬂood risk management.
