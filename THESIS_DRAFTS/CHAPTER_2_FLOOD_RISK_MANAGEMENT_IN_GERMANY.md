## Flood Risk Management in Germany

Flood risk management in Germany has changed substantially over the last decades. While earlier approaches were dominated by technical flood protection and the attempt to control water mainly through structural measures, more recent debates and policy developments increasingly frame flooding as a broader risk management problem. This shift was not purely conceptual, but strongly event-driven. According to Thieken et al. (2016), the 2002 flood marked an important reorientation toward integrated flood risk management in Germany. A similar point is made by Sairam et al. (2021), who note that the major events of 1993, 1995, and especially 2002 triggered policy decisions in favor of more systemic approaches. In this context, the European Floods Directive became a key institutional milestone, because it required member states to identify significant flood risks and prepare flood risk management plans. Germany implemented this broader orientation within its national legal and planning framework, which means that flood risk management today has to be understood as a combination of hazard assessment, spatial planning, emergency management, damage reduction, and long-term adaptation rather than as flood defence alone (Sairam et al., 2021; Thieken et al., 2016).

The importance of this shift becomes clearer when considering the role of major flood events in German policy learning. Thieken et al. (2016) describe the 2002 flood as the most expensive natural hazard event in Germany up to that point, with damage of EUR 11.6 billion and 21 fatalities. The 2013 flood again caused very high losses, estimated at EUR 6 to 8 billion, but it also demonstrated that some improvements had already taken effect. Thieken et al. (2016) argue that by 2013 Germany had improved flood warnings, disaster response coordination, property-level mitigation, and the maintenance of flood defence systems. In a similar way, Grabs (2016) presents the Elbe floods of 2002 and 2013 as key triggers for the Elbe Flood Protection Action Plan and as an important moment in the broader acceptance of integrated flood risk management. These events therefore matter not only because they caused severe damage, but because they exposed weaknesses in existing institutions and made flood governance a long-term political issue. Germany is thus a useful case because its current flood risk management system emerged through repeated extreme events, institutional learning, and ongoing policy adjustment.

A central characteristic of German flood risk management is that it operates across multiple governance levels and across different types of measures. At the European level, the Floods Directive created a common framework, while national and subnational actors remain responsible for implementation, planning, and financing in practice (Sairam et al., 2021). This multi-level structure is important because it means that flood risk management outcomes are shaped through coordination problems, regional variation, and institutional capacity, not only through technical necessity. At the same time, the German debate has moved beyond a narrow focus on dikes and flood walls. Grabs (2016) emphasizes that integrated flood management includes a mix of structural and non-structural measures, including warning systems, land-use regulation, reactivation of former floodplains, and broader adaptation strategies. Thieken et al. (2016) likewise stress the importance of spatial planning, preparedness, and public involvement. More recent work also shows that flood risk management increasingly includes private and semi-private dimensions, such as insurance, household mitigation, and municipality-level communication. Surminski and Thieken (2017) argue that insurance should be seen as part of a broader anticipatory flood risk management field, while Osberghaus (2015) shows that private mitigation remains important because public protection cannot eliminate flood risk entirely.

Despite this broader framing, German flood risk management still contains important tensions. On the one hand, public policy increasingly recognizes that absolute protection is neither technically nor economically feasible. Grabs (2016) explicitly argues that structural measures can mitigate flood hazards but cannot eliminate them, and that relying on engineering solutions alone may even increase residual risk and vulnerability in the event of failure. On the other hand, structural protection remains politically central because it is visible, state-led, and strongly associated with public safety. This creates a persistent tension between reactive and anticipatory approaches. Surminski and Thieken (2017) describe flood risk management in Germany and England as still largely reactive and event-driven, even though anticipatory approaches are widely discussed. Recent research on household adaptation in Germany supports this interpretation. Osberghaus (2015) and Dillenardt and Thieken (2025) both show that private adaptation, communication, and individual preparedness are important, but unevenly developed. In other words, integrated flood risk management has expanded conceptually, yet in practice it remains unevenly realized and still heavily shaped by crisis response, historical legacies, and institutional path dependencies.

For this thesis, the German context is therefore relevant in two ways. First, it provides an institutional setting in which flood risk management is formally broad enough to include hazard reduction, vulnerability reduction, planning, and preparedness. Second, it remains a setting in which structural protection infrastructure continues to play a major political and practical role. This duality is important for a study that examines the distribution of flood exposure and flood protection in the Elbe context. If German flood risk management is both integrated in principle and uneven in practice, then questions of distribution become unavoidable. The relevant issue is not only whether protection exists, but how its benefits and the remaining burdens of exposure are distributed across different municipalities and socio-economic conditions. In this sense, Germany is not merely the national background of the thesis, but the governance context that makes the central research questions analytically meaningful.
