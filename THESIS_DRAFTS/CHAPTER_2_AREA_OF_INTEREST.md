## Area of Interest

The Elbe is one of the most important river systems in Germany and has long been a central space of flood risk management, settlement development, and political intervention. In the context of this thesis, the Elbe is not treated merely as a hydrological basin, but as a socio-spatial corridor in which flood dynamics, settlement patterns, and public protection strategies intersect. This perspective is particularly relevant because repeated major flood events have made the Elbe one of the most visible river systems in German flood policy. Grabs (2016) highlights the Elbe floods of 2002 and 2013 as key triggers for the Elbe Flood Protection Action Plan, while Jüpner (2018) underlines that the Elbe flood disaster of 2013 again demonstrated both the destructive potential of extreme floods and the limits of technical protection. In this sense, the Elbe is not just an empirically convenient case. It is a river corridor in which the relationships between hazard, protection, and social conditions can be observed especially clearly.

Historically, the Elbe has been shaped by a long interaction between settlement, river engineering, and flood experience. Jüpner (2018) shows that settlement along the Elbe originally concentrated on relatively elevated areas near the river, as in Magdeburg, where settlement historically remained more than 15 m above mean river level. Over time, however, population growth, land-use pressure, and hydraulic engineering transformed the river into an increasingly engineered system. According to Jüpner (2018), more than 500 km of the Elbe in Germany are now almost completely lined by dikes, and more than 83% of the natural floodplains along the river have been lost. This is highly relevant for the present study because it illustrates that the Elbe is not a “natural” floodplain in any simple sense. Instead, it is a heavily modified river landscape in which exposure, protection, and residual risk are historically co-produced. Grabs (2016) makes a similar point by arguing that integrated flood management in the Elbe context must move beyond technical flood control alone and combine structural and non-structural strategies. The Elbe therefore offers an especially useful setting for examining how flood exposure and flood protection are embedded in a broader socio-political landscape.

The Elbe is also a strong case because it has repeatedly been affected by severe flood events in recent decades. The major floods of 2002 and 2013 are especially important in this regard, but they are not the only events that matter. Barendrecht et al. (2018) discuss the Dresden case and show that the damage in 2013 was lower than in 2002 even though flood risk remained substantial, which they interpret as evidence that societal awareness and preparedness had changed after the earlier event. Jüpner (2018) likewise argues that the 2013 flood reopened debates on how flood risk should be managed, especially because two dike failures occurred and because areas assumed to be protected still suffered major damage. Earlier work by Lindenschmidt et al. (2006) further shows that large-basin risk assessment in the Elbe and Mulde context is methodologically demanding and was, at that time, still in its infancy. These studies are important for the present thesis because they show that the Elbe is characterized not only by repeated flood exposure, but also by repeated institutional and social learning. The area of interest is therefore not simply flood-prone; it is a landscape in which flood events have continuously reshaped risk perception, protection strategies, and governance debates.

At the same time, the Elbe corridor is not only hydrologically heterogeneous, but also socially and economically uneven. This is crucial for the logic of the thesis. The river crosses regions that are embedded in broader East-West and urban-rural disparities within Germany. The 2024 report *Ost und West. Frei, vereint und unvollkommen* emphasizes that the social and economic transformation of Germany after reunification remains incomplete and that eastern and western parts of the country still differ in important structural respects (Der Beauftragte der Bundesregierung für Ostdeutschland, 2024). A similar picture emerges from the broader regional disparity literature. Heider et al. (2023) argue that existing socio-economic disparities may intensify if effective preventive measures are lacking and stress the importance of reliable indicators for identifying emerging patterns of inequality. Helbig (2023), in turn, shows that socio-spatial inequality in German cities is expressed through persistent concentrations of poverty, wealth, education, and social segregation. For the purposes of this thesis, these sources are useful because they show that the Elbe corridor runs through municipalities with very different social and economic profiles. The study area therefore cannot be understood only in terms of river morphology or floodplains. It must also be understood as a socially differentiated space in which vulnerability and adaptive capacity are unevenly distributed.

This socio-spatial heterogeneity is one of the main reasons why the Elbe is particularly suitable for a justice-oriented flood study. A flood-prone corridor that crosses municipalities with differing income structures, household profiles, demographic characteristics, and accessibility conditions creates a setting in which distributive questions become highly relevant. The issue is not only whether flood hazard exists, but whether exposure and protection are distributed in ways that align with or contradict broader social inequalities. Odersky and Löffler (2024) show for the 2021 floods in Germany that flood exposure can be clearly regressive at the local level, with affected neighborhoods displaying substantially lower average incomes than nearby unaffected ones. Although their study focuses on a different flood event and spatial setup, it reinforces the broader relevance of distributional analysis in Germany. Fekete (2010) similarly argues that social vulnerability analysis is important because it reveals hidden strengths and weaknesses of society in relation to river floods and because only few German studies had directly addressed these hidden social dimensions. Against this background, the Elbe becomes analytically valuable not simply because it is flood-prone, but because it combines repeated flood experience, a long history of structural intervention, and a socially differentiated regional setting. This combination makes it particularly well suited for investigating how flood hazard, exposure, and protection relate to socially relevant factors in the Elbe river basin.

A final conceptual clarification is important here. While the thesis remains framed in terms of the Elbe river basin, the empirical analysis later focuses on the flood-relevant corridor along the Elbe rather than on the full basin as a uniform analytical space. This distinction is important because not all municipalities that are hydrologically part of the basin are equally relevant for flood exposure analysis. However, the broader basin framing remains useful at the conceptual level because it anchors the study in the long-standing governance, planning, and river-management context of the Elbe as a major German river system. In other words, the Elbe basin provides the historical and institutional frame, whereas the corridor provides the empirically flood-relevant space. This combination allows the thesis to remain faithful to its title and research questions while still focusing analytically on the municipalities where flood hazard, exposure, and potential protection effects are most meaningful to compare.
