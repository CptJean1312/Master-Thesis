# 1. INTRODUCTION

## Overall job of Chapter 1
- Establish that floods are not only hydrological hazards but socially and politically structured risks.
- Narrow from global/European relevance to German relevance, then to the Elbe case.
- Introduce justice as the key lens.
- Show the missing link in the literature: municipality-level distributive analysis of flood exposure and protection in the Elbe context.
- End with the exact research questions and a cautious working hypothesis.

## Red thread of the whole chapter
- Start with the broad importance of floods under climate change.
- Then move to the fact that flood losses, exposure, and protection are not socially neutral.
- Then show that justice questions in flood risk management are real but underexamined empirically.
- Then identify the specific gap for Germany / the Elbe.
- Then state exactly what your thesis contributes.

## One-sentence chapter logic
- Flood risk is rising and socially uneven; therefore, it is necessary to examine whether flood exposure and flood protection are distributed fairly across socio-economic groups in the Elbe river basin.

## Things Chapter 1 must not do
- Do **not** go deep into methods.
- Do **not** fully explain the PCA here.
- Do **not** already defend every data decision here.
- Do **not** overclaim that you can prove intentional injustice.
- Do **not** promise more than the design can do; frame the thesis as an analysis of distributive patterns.

## Recommended internal paragraph rhythm for the whole chapter
- `MOTIVATION`: 3 to 4 paragraphs
- `CLIMATE CHANGE, FLOODS & JUSTICE`: 2 to 3 paragraphs
- `RESEARCH GAP & OBJECTIVE`: 2 to 3 paragraphs
- `RESEARCH QUESTIONS & HYPOTHESIS`: 1 to 2 paragraphs

## MOTIVATION

### Core function of this subsection
- Convince the reader that the topic matters.
- Build urgency, relevance, and case selection logic.
- Make the reader care about the distributional question before the formal justice framing begins.

### Subsection red thread
- Floods are major and increasing hazards.
- Climate change and socio-economic change increase future damages.
- Germany is highly relevant because of major flood events and extensive FRM responses.
- The Elbe is a strong case because it combines severe flood history with large-scale protection planning and socio-economic heterogeneity.
- Therefore, the key question is not only whether floods occur, but how exposure and protection are distributed.

### Suggested paragraph structure

#### Paragraph 1 - Floods matter at scale
**Goal**
- Open with the general significance of floods.

**Core claims to make**
- Flooding is among the most consequential natural hazards globally and in Europe.
- Exposure to flood hazards has increased over time and is projected to keep increasing.
- This is not a niche or local problem.

**Evidence / numbers you can use**
- `Global exposure to river and coastal flooding...` says flooding affects "several million people globally each year".
- Same paper estimates global flood exposure at `46 trillion USD` in 2010 and `80 trillion USD` in 2050.
- `Climate change and river floods in the European Union...` projects expected annual damages from `€5.5 billion/year` to `€98 billion/year` by the 2080s under no-adaptation conditions.

**Use these sources here**
- Must-have:
  - `Global exposure to river and coastal flooding: Long term trends and changes`
  - `Climate change and river floods in the European Union: Socio-economic consequences and the costs and benefits of adaptation`

**Directly usable quote hooks**
- `Global exposure...`, p.1: "affecting several million people globally each year"
- `Climate change and river floods in the European Union...`, p.1: "current levels of protection are kept constant"
- `Climate change and river floods in the European Union...`, p.1: "expected annual damages of €5.5 billion/year are projected to reach €98 billion/year"

**What to write after the evidence**
- A 1-2 sentence interpretation that rising flood relevance is driven by both physical and socio-economic change.
- Then a transition: this makes adaptation and flood risk management increasingly central public tasks.

**Transition out of paragraph**
- Move from "floods are important" to "how societies manage floods matters".

#### Paragraph 2 - Climate change makes flood governance more urgent
**Goal**
- Link flood relevance directly to climate change and adaptation.

**Core claims to make**
- Climate change intensifies the importance of flood risk governance.
- Adaptation and protection strategies are now central, not optional.
- The debate is not only about hazard change, but also about what kinds of protection societies choose.

**Evidence / numbers you can use**

**Use these sources here**

**Interpretive note**

- Do **not** introduce justice fully yet; just prepare for it.

**Core claims to make**

**Use these sources here**

**Interpretive note**
- Use the Germany/Elbe discussion to justify case relevance, not to dump historical detail.
- The point is: the Elbe is a strong place to study flood governance, exposure, and protection.

**Transition out of paragraph**
- Move from case importance to social relevance: if the Elbe is central to German flood governance, the next question is who benefits from that governance and who remains exposed.

**Interpretive note**
- This paragraph should end with the actual social puzzle:
  - not only how much flood risk exists,
  - but whether exposure and protection are socially differentiated.

**Transition to next subsection**

  - if flood risks and flood responses are socially differentiated, then the issue is one of justice as much as one of hazard management.

## CLIMATE CHANGE, FLOODS & JUSTICE

### Core function of this subsection
- Introduce the normative lens.
- Explain why justice belongs in a flood thesis.
- Define what kind of justice matters most here: distributive justice.

### Subsection red thread
- Climate change increases the stakes of adaptation.
- Flood protection and flood governance distribute benefits and burdens.
- That makes justice an intrinsic, not external, part of flood risk management.
- This thesis focuses on the distributive side of that problem.

### Suggested paragraph structure

#### Paragraph 1 - Why climate adaptation is also a justice issue
**Goal**
- Connect climate change and adaptation to fairness.

**Core claims to make**
- As climate impacts intensify, adaptation decisions allocate resources, risks, and benefits.
- Flood governance therefore always has a social distributional dimension.
- The question is not only whether protection is effective, but for whom it is effective.

**Use these sources here**
- Must-have:
  - `Fair strategies to tackle unfair risks? Justice considerations within flood risk management`
  - `Justice and flood risk management: reflecting on different approaches to distribute and allocate flood risk management in Europe`
  - `Environmental justice and redistribution`

**Directly usable quote hooks**
- `Fair strategies...`, p.1: "This literature review investigates how and why justice is considered in flood risk management"
- `Fair strategies...`, p.1: distinction between "distributive and procedural justice"
- `Justice and flood risk management...`, p.1: "As protection against flood risks becomes increasingly difficult, dilemmas of justice emerge"
- `Justice and flood risk management...`, p.1: "some benefits from flood protection measures whereas others loose"
- `Environmental justice and redistribution`, p.1: "flood prevention can however lead to environmental injustice"

**Interpretive note**
- Make this paragraph the conceptual pivot from the motivation to the justice frame.
- You do not need to explain every justice tradition yet; just establish why justice is relevant.

**Transition out of paragraph**
- Move from general climate adaptation justice to flood-specific distributive injustice.

#### Paragraph 2 - Flood risk management is not socially neutral
**Goal**
- Show that FRM can produce or reproduce uneven outcomes.

**Core claims to make**
- Flood protection redistributes safety, exposure, and sometimes even flood water.
- The distribution of protection measures is therefore inherently political and socially consequential.
- Justice problems arise in both outcomes and decision processes, but your thesis concentrates on outcomes.

**Use these sources here**
- Must-have:
  - `Environmental justice and redistribution`
  - `Natural and imposed injustices...`
  - `Justice and flood risk management...`
- Optional:
  - `Beyond‘just’ flood risk management...`

**Directly usable quote hooks**
- `Environmental justice and redistribution`, p.1: "unjust floodwater redistribution"
- `Natural and imposed injustices...`, p.1: "flooding is not fair per se"
- `Natural and imposed injustices...`, p.2: fairness must consider both those "at risk of flooding" and "the taxpayer"
- `Justice and flood risk management...`, p.1: "What’s the right thing to protect"

**Interpretive note**
- This is where you can briefly distinguish:
  - procedural justice = how decisions are made
  - distributive justice = who gets protected / who remains exposed
- Then state clearly: this thesis focuses mainly on distributive justice.

**Transition out of paragraph**
- Move from theoretical justice questions to the empirical issue of socially differentiated flood exposure.

#### Paragraph 3 - Bridge to the empirical thesis problem
**Goal**
- Close the subsection by pointing to exposure, vulnerability, and protection as empirical dimensions of justice.

**Core claims to make**
- Justice in flood governance can be studied through the spatial distribution of exposure and protection relative to socio-economic conditions.
- Social vulnerability matters because equal hazard does not imply equal social consequences.
- Therefore, distributive patterns must be examined together with social structure.

**Use these sources here**
- Must-have:

## RESEARCH GAP & OBJECTIVE

### Core function of this subsection
- Turn the conceptual discussion into a concrete academic niche.
- Show exactly what previous literature has and has not done.
- State your contribution precisely.

### Subsection red thread
- There is evidence that flood exposure can be socially uneven.
- There are strong justice debates in FRM.
- But municipality-level, German, riverine, corridor-based distributive analyses remain limited.
- Your thesis addresses this gap by linking flood exposure / protection questions to socio-economic vulnerability in the Elbe case.

### Suggested paragraph structure

#### Paragraph 1 - What the literature already shows
**Goal**
- Demonstrate that social inequality in flood exposure is a real research field, not a fabricated gap.

**Core claims to make**
- Several studies show socially differentiated flood exposure in different country contexts.
- These findings justify treating flood exposure as a social distribution question.
- The scale of analysis matters for what inequalities become visible.

**Use these sources here**
- Must-have:
  - `Environmental Inequalities in Flood Exposure: A Matter of Scale`
  - `Disparities of population exposed to flood hazards in the United States`
  - `Inequalities in exposure and awareness of flood risk in England and Wales`
  - `Social disparities in neighborhood flood exposure in 44,698 urban neighborhoods in Latin America`

**Directly usable quote hooks**
- `Environmental Inequalities in Flood Exposure...`, p.1: "little is known on inequalities for riverine floods"
- `Environmental Inequalities in Flood Exposure...`, p.1: the relation varies with the "spatial scale considered"
- `Disparities of population exposed...`, p.2: the study provides a "nationwide county-based assessment" of exposure disparities

**Interpretive note**
- Be careful here: do not pretend nobody has ever studied floods and inequality.

**Transition out of paragraph**

## RESEARCH QUESTIONS & HYPOTHESIS

### Core function of this subsection
- Lock the chapter into a clear analytical program.
- Present exactly what the thesis asks and expects.

### Absolute rule
- Keep the research questions exactly as fixed in `/Users/maxi_161/Desktop/UNI/Master/THESIS/THESIS.docx`.

### Research questions to insert exactly
1. `How is flood hazard and exposure distributed across socio-economic groups in the Elbe River basin?`
2. `What role does flood protection infrastructure play in shaping this distribution?`

### Working hypothesis to insert
- `Socio-economically disadvantaged regions, especially in eastern Germany, receive less protection.`

### How to unpack the research questions right after listing them
**Point 1 - unpack RQ1**
- Explain that this question is about distributive patterning of hazard/exposure.
- Clarify that exposure is treated as spatially measurable and socially differentiable.
- Explain that socio-economic groups are proxied through municipality-level indicators of deprivation, age, household structure, and accessibility/adaptive capacity.

**Point 2 - unpack RQ2**
- Explain that this question addresses the shaping role of protection infrastructure or protection-related patterns in the distribution of risk.
- Keep the wording broad enough that the fixed title/RQ remain compatible with the evolving operationalization.
- Do not overcommit to one single protection dataset in Chapter 1.

**Point 3 - qualify the hypothesis carefully**
- The hypothesis is directional, not deterministic.
- The thesis evaluates distributive patterns, not intentional discrimination.
- Alternative explanations such as topography, settlement history, and hydrological conditions remain possible and will be discussed later.
